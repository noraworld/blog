## Capitalism Doesn't Always Reflect Effort or Struggle

From a young age, people study hard, go to a prestigious college, work on their theses despite difficulties, graduate, and finally secure jobs at top companies after fierce competition. Yet, in some cases, soap hostesses at upscale establishments tend to earn more on average.

Did these hostesses struggle harder than the so-called elites to secure their jobs and status? Not necessarily. These nightlife jobs often value natural looks, which weigh heavily in the industry.

Appearance isn't everything, but in jobs where it matters (like nightlife jobs), one can earn more than those in prestigious positions. Also, I'm not saying that these jobs are easy money, but those with good natural looks might earn a lot without much struggle (not everyone, of course!).

In this capitalist society, the idea that "earning money is justice" seems questionable. Society often thinks that those who can't earn are not trying hard enough. Even without this sentiment, capitalism means not earning money results in a lesser quality of life.

### ⚠️ It's Not About Discriminating Jobs

This isn't about saying, "night jobs make easy money and it's unfair." It's just an example; it doesn't have to be about corporate employees and soap hostesses.

Night jobs are generally considered high-earning, don't require special qualifications, and face less competition. In contrast, in developed countries, society often idealizes top companies, leading to fierce competition and the need for intense study, which makes it easy to compare these two examples.

I'm aware that "Soap hostesses also need hospitality and interpersonal skills; it's not just about looks. And they may encounter difficult customers," but that's not the point here. I'd like you to understand that I have no prejudice against nighttime jobs (and if you do think so, feel free to replace these examples with others that convey the intent).

Earning in these jobs is a talent, and if that's your passion, great. I'm not criticizing. I'm just saying striving based solely on financial values can feel futile compared to those with natural talents or looks.

## Money Doesn't Have Colors

Here's another example. Imagine we meet for the first time, and I show you a $100 bill. Would you know how I got it?

If you knew my job, you'd think I earned it by working, which makes sense. Even without knowing, living in this capitalist society, you'd believe it's from work.

But there's no guarantee. On my way to meet you, I could have secretly taken it from an unaware granny. If nobody notices, including the granny, I'd never get caught.

You might think, "That's extreme; you can't do that often." But some cases, like shady job schemes or untouchable overseas crime syndicates, show such earnings exist. Earnings from unchecked crime are real.

As the saying goes, "money has no color." Even $100 bills obtained (unjustly) this way hold the same value and are treated the same as money earned through hard work.

Even without physical cash, with cashless payments on the rise, internet scams can swindle money, too. Such bank balances, despite their sources, hold the same value as those earned through hard work.

Money is an unclear medium, a piece of paper, or bank balance numbers. Yet in capitalism, life requires it for a good or even sustainable living (though basic living rights are somewhat guaranteed...).

Although I mentioned criminal acts, even without them, hard work can't exceed those with privilege. Even if hard work somehow leads you to great wealth, it may not be worth the effort compared to those comfortably enjoying inherited wealth.

The same goes for growing up in a wealthy family. Some enjoy luxury, while others face poverty, a capitalist disparity (born by capitalism).

## Rich People Are Just Lucky

You might say, "If you're complaining, why not start a business and become rich?" but that's capitalist ideology rearing its head.

It's because we have capitalism and freedom of occupation that people can casually say, "be an entrepreneur." Yet, humans have strengths and weaknesses, and not everyone succeeds as an entrepreneur. It's not just about hard work; natural abilities play a role (which I'll discuss later).

Think of artists or athletes. Can you, by working hard, become as popular as Yoji Kenshi? Or as accomplished as Shohei Ohtani? I believe many would find this challenging, seeing that hard work alone can't break certain barriers.

The same idea applies to being an entrepreneur. Many might have studied harder than others yet lost to those naturally gifted in academics.

I'm not saying one can't improve or be successful without talent. With proper effort, anyone can achieve something. But everyone has limits, and some may not want to pursue wealth or entrepreneurship.

If everyone wanted "big money" and started businesses, there'd be no one growing crops or building houses, and society would crumble. Some people love farming and have unique talents for it. Just like business talents exist.

But in capitalism, different jobs create immense economic differences regardless of skill or effort. Even if skilled entrepreneurs contribute greatly, without farmers, life wouldn't sustain.

Capitalism may favor entrepreneurs economically. But in another time, like a warring state era, a lord would hold such power, using strategy or military skills. Successful businesspeople in today's world wouldn't fare the same back then.

In short, it's pure luck. By chance, someone might have good looks and no issues with nightlife work, earning as much as, if not more than, so-called "elite" workers without much effort. Others grow up in wealth, living richly without a struggle. Or, someone might have entrepreneurial gifts and the fortune of being born in today's pro-capitalism era, allowing them to utilize said talents to earn big.

Certainly, those born with these traits and situations face unique struggles, but in a capitalistic world where "earning is justice," it feels unfair.

### Exploitation of Workers by Capitalists

Those who couldn't take such paths, especially the many employed in Japan, earn their wages or salaries as compensation for their labor. Yet, they don't get paid what their labor is actually worth.

Some data suggests necessary labor time and surplus labor time are about equal. Ideally, this means working half of an 8-hour day should suffice, yet they work double that to earn the same wage. They're worked twice as hard for the same pay – without overtime.

If they work overtime, surplus labor time increases even more. This is capitalist exploitation of workers. Frankly, one could say half of a salaried workday could be spent slacking and they’d still break even at best.

Although there's freedom of occupation, not everyone can become a capitalist. This ties back, partially, to natural talent beyond mere effort.

### Capitalism's Frustration Surpassing Absolute Monarchy

So if capitalism means the wealthy live lavishly while others' wealth is absorbed, it's akin to medieval times with monarchy. It was at least easier to give up when status couldn't be earned by effort – perhaps that was even easier. Now, capitalists replace monarchs, offering the illusion of fairness ("Stop complaining, work harder"), perhaps making today's situation worse.

Belief in this illusion leads to much effort with little gain, creating unfortunate situations. Even realizing this fosters dissatisfaction labeled as "loser's whining." If you were resigned to never being a king or noble, at least then, perhaps useless effort in slim possibilities wouldn't bring more misery, and people might seek happiness by other means.

### "Try harder" Stealth Discriminators

I don't mean to say effort is bad. If effort itself is enjoyable, that's great. But if it brings suffering, maybe it's worth questioning. Even if hard work leads to wealth, happiness isn't guaranteed.

The real problem is people who lecture others for supposedly not trying hard enough. Honestly, it seems to me those who say such things really believe everything is thanks to their own hard work alone without acknowledging the role of luck or inherent ability in their success (effort itself is influenced by genetics). It's like telling a struggling person they should be ashamed of themselves.

Some say, "Life isn’t sweet enough to live doing what you love," but I think it's more accurate to say, "Life isn't sweet enough to find happiness doing what you don’t love." Let's stop evaluating life solely based on economic success.

This is a form of discrimination. Even if racism or disability discrimination becomes unacceptable, there will always be those who seek new targets. "If you want money, put in the effort to earn it" is now a target.

Capitalism as luck won an Ig Nobel Prize, and effort is heavily influenced by genetics. How is saying “make an effort” not discrimination like "170 cm below has no rights" demeans height and discriminates against others?

Again, I don't insist efforts are useless or expect special treatment for those unskilled in them. But if anyone says "It’s just excuses," or "You’re just evading," then they are no different from **racists**. They should be criticized. It's one thing not to make excuses yourself. But it's not okay to impose or preach to others like it's a standard of judgment.

People who lecture about "not trying hard enough" should expect disdain and dislike. Recognize their distorted sense of justice. Depression sufferers don't deserve to be called lazy, just as terminal cancer patients shouldn't be told to tough it out. Stop the discrimination.

### Effort Believers

Economically successful people always say they worked hard, so when someone says "It's all luck," or "Effort is mostly genetic," some might feel offended like their hard work is belittled. Yet even the ability to work hard is a blessing from above, and without the timing of using one's skills in a "profitable job," how can they believe effort alone brought success? Could you switch lives with those socially disadvantaged and say the same?

All this praise about effort, yet without others, no amount of effort would lead to success. Without people to buy your products, how can you get rich? You shine because of others. Is making money through effort so praiseworthy?

## A World Where Hard Work Might Not Pay Off

No matter how high your education level, no matter the company you work for, no matter how hard you try, sometimes, you can't outperform those who've barely struggled (again, not saying all soap hostesses or entrepreneurs don't struggle). Money you earn is just numbers, their source unknown. Crime could get you the same things, or life's conditions based on birth and talent decide fate.

If your passion isn't purely about earning (increasing bank account numbers), something about the work itself, then I don’t understand why anyone would want to "try hard" in capitalism. Yet those who don't try face mockery, ridicule, criticism, scolding. Capitalism – what a wonderful system. Everyone’s trying hard, aren't they?

### Nobody Cares About Good or Bad Creations

Writing too – there are people who earn from that. They must have readers, and even if their writings aren't great, they likely get read because "that person wrote it." Putting aside if this article is worthy, even if a great article is written, if it's unknown, it won't be read. In the end, work quality doesn't always correlate with recognition.

Focusing solely on earnings won't bring happiness. If anything, finding work that feels more like play than labor might be the path to happiness, but I haven’t found that work yet, and my writing isn’t profitable. So, I engage in employment that isn't what I desire, regardless of its societal prestige, and it doesn't make me happy.

From financial freedom to mental freedom. Will such an ideal society ever come? Even if it does, 20 or 30 years later is too late; I'd be near the end of my working life anyway... Life feels so futile...

### The Endless Loop of a Miserable Loser

On the flip side, thinking money is just paper (just balance numbers) lends to a carefree approach of living without regard for expense – living in the moment. However, coming from a poor background where "money is important" and "it's a waste to use it like that" were instilled from childhood makes that hard. I also worry about the future, thinking spending recklessly could lead to issues down the road.

And with no choice but to live in capitalism, the idea that it’s unfair lingers and results in feelings like questioning spending on someone like a soap hostess worth such high amounts, leading to that cycle of thoughts like, "Then why go to these places at all?" comes around, but I did have a lifetime of not being romantically successful, feeling without any connections, leading to unresolved feelings, creating this loop.

Economic failure, social failure, family failure, romantic failure, thinking failure... What's with this life??
