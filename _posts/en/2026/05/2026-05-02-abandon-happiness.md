## The Trap of Metaphors
When told, "Stop comparing everything to the Tokyo Dome!" someone might agree and say, "You're right, using the Tokyo Dome as an example isn't good. Here's how I would explain it using the Tokyo Dome!" This shows they don't truly understand the idea of "not using the Tokyo Dome as a metaphor."

## The Impulse Trap
Deciding on impulse might save you from regret, but thinking everything through makes you miss great chances. However, the future is unknown and worrying is pointless. Yet, that doesn't mean "always act impulsively without thinking" is a good idea either.

## The Attachment Trap
Buddha said, "Letting go of attachment frees you from suffering." If someone says, "Got it! Letting go of attachment frees you!" and then becomes obsessed with letting go, they won’t find relief from suffering.

Abandoning the belief "being rich brings happiness" might make you happy, but intentionally abandoning the desire to earn money doesn't guarantee happiness. Though being rich doesn't always mean happiness, it might be true for some, or money might not be what anyone is attached to.

For such people, releasing pressure about earning money could inadvertently lead to more wealth. Yet, if feeling pressure to release that pressure, wealth might not increase and happiness might remain elusive.

Even ignoring someone annoying might free you from suffering, but worrying about ignoring them only brings more suffering.

Giving up television to focus on what matters might make you want to watch more and ultimately be distracting. However, worrying about not bothering over these distractions leads nowhere and.........

## The Nature Trap
Laozi said, "Things go well if done without forcing." If one thinks, "Got it, things go well if not forcing!” and tries hard not to force things, it doesn’t go well.

He also said, "You become yourself by not worrying about who you are." When someone becomes obsessed with not being obsessed about identity, they become less of who they truly are.

"Giving up desires makes things go well" doesn't mean having the desire to give up desires makes things go well either.

## The Abandonment Trap
Sometimes, nothing seems to come after hard work. When finally giving up, things once strived for manifest naturally.

However, intentionally giving up with expectations to receive doesn't guarantee fulfillment. Only when genuinely accepting either outcome, true desires might appear, or they might not.

## The Science Trap
Once, I believed blindly in science and practiced meditation regularly hearing it'd stabilize my mind. But nothing changed because I expected too much. Thinking "meditation doesn’t work" ruins any potential benefit.

It's important to think "oh well" if results don't show. But unknowingly hoping for results won’t work. Expectations and rewards shouldn’t be our focal point even if, or when, they do appear. Disappointment should not follow any fruitless attempts, as a certain resilience is necessary.

This thinking applies beyond meditation, like medicine or therapy. Believing healing stabilizes mentally often leads nowhere but temporary solace, in turn, worsening disappointment.

⚠️ **This doesn’t render medication or treatment meaningless. In overwhelming distress, medication is indeed advisable. Always prioritize professional medical advice.**

## The Reliance Trap
Thinking, "I'm doomed for happiness as I confess my frailty, seeking salvation from Buddha. Even a wretched soul deserves happiness, doesn't it?" ends guilt and finds salvation. Truly, "the worse one feels, the more they're saved."

But thinking "Since salvation favors bad people, embracing evil brings it!" doesn’t hold true either.

Interestingly, "bad people" in the True Pure Land Buddhism means "those who realize they can’t give up their earthly desires on their own" while "good people" implies "those who believe they can accomplish things alone."

So, pursuing evil thinking it works, believing it’s self-empowerment doesn’t lead to salvation. It implies "If one can achieve happiness alone, who needs salvation? Proceed!" instead.

Enunciating "Namu Amida Butsu" while clasping hands alone doesn’t deliver salvation. It’s a deep philosophy (Buddhism), not mere belief in divine forces.

## The Happiness Trap
Achieving something in life (wealth, fame, attraction) may equate to happiness, though not necessarily. True self might find immense happiness in a mundane life.

If true, unnecessary accomplishments aren't needed. Savoring daily simplicity might bring happiness, leading eventually to wealth, fame, or attraction unexpectedly.

Yet, expecting them while years pass fails understanding "happiness." Complaints derive from misunderstanding happiness if expectations aren't met post embracing simplicity.

True happiness means accepting outcomes as they unravel, finding contentment with or without changes.

Ultimately, it's okay not to be wealthy, to be poor...even being rich yet distrustful due to others' motives is fine. Not being famous, losing prior fame, or facing scandals means embracing it all without insistence on outcomes.

But forcefully accepting fate doesn’t yield happiness. Merely thinking it feeds expectations; thus, stop considering it. Ceasing such deliberations aggrandizes expectations...

## The Trap of “The Trap of ‘The Trap of the Trap’”
Everything, happiness or ideals included, defies accurate word expression. You can only describe them as "not this, not that."

Awareness makes enlightenment neither easy nor hard, nor "easy yet hard," nor "not easy nor hard."

For happiness, don't strive for happiness, but striving not to never results in it. Avoid pondering over not striving, along with pondering over not pondering,

Infinite concepts elude words. Only life experience unveils sensations. Good luck.
