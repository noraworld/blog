## Putting Yourself Under a Spell
Continuing from ['This world is an illusion. Just given a fake life. Nevertheless, thinking otherwise is what 'living' means.'](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n7e7607a3a348). I wasn't quite finished with my thoughts, so here I am writing more.

Even after posting that article, the fear of a massive earthquake or death hasn't disappeared. In fact, I never had such fear before, not when I was completely unaware of Buddhism or Eastern Philosophy. But pondering these things has only increased my fear. It's like the exact opposite of what Buddhist teachings suggest.

And after writing it, I concluded with "believe in the beliefs you want to believe," and suddenly I felt my body get really light. My body seemed strangely fluffy, making it hard to feel grounded. "Feeling lighter" might sound good, but it was so light that it felt like I might float into the afterlife. Accepting that without resistance feels like what true Nirvana is. But, if that's the case, I'd end up dying, which is definitely not good, and fighting against that is like "clinging to life" which Buddha calls attachment. It makes me think, "Let's let go of attachments and accept death." But then again, if this universe is an illusion—just a dream the consciousness of 'me' sees—then maybe living itself was false from the start, and death might actually have happened... 

But no matter how much I think, I know these are just unprovable beliefs. To test it, I'd have to actually die, but that would be just suicide in this world's terms. And assuming this real world is right, Buddhism might seem like it's allowing suicide, which just seems unlikely... then again, thinking so might mean this world is fiction (an illusion by 'my' consciousness)...

If Brahman (the universe) and Atman ('me') are the ultimate truth, recognizing that Buddhist teachings are a belief and the only release from suffering is at death could lead to the universe collapsing the next day or 50 years later. If it's the latter, it just seems I died of old age, making it more realistic in a general sense. Both are beliefs, and there's no way to find the truth.

So there's nothing we can do. If interpreted by "common sense" (forgetting philosophy for a moment), humans will die someday anyway. If I'm going to die regardless, up to that natural moment, I should choose beliefs that are convenient and make things easier. Thinking this way, everything might be a belief, but within that, you should adopt beliefs that make you feel free, happy, and free of worries or suffering. Since there's nothing that can be conclusively proven not to be a belief, choosing some belief is the only way to live. So, **consider thoughts or events that cause anger, fear, anxiety, or worry as beliefs and decide that fun and happy thoughts are real, not just beliefs.**

When you think "I'm worried about money and the future" or "I'm scared of big earthquakes or dying," just think, "That's a belief!" Then, when something like "Bungee jumping is scary, but it might be fun if I try" seems like a belief (only feeling the fear of falling), tell yourself, "That's not a belief!" If it turns out you only felt fear, remember that can be a belief too (later thinking, "Bungee jumping was scary, but even being scared was a good memory"). Just interpret as conveniently as possible and truly believe it.

## The Secret of Secret Buddhism
One branch of Buddhism is 'Esoteric Buddhism,' or 'Secret Buddhism.' As the name suggests, its details are kept secret. You can find it online, but the doctrine and actual practices are a mystery, so ordinary people know little.

But I have a theory. I record my philosophical journey as accurately as possible. If someone read it, they might say, "Ah, enlightenment feels like that."

But, enlightenment needs to be experienced, not just understood with words. Simply knowing doesn't equate to actual enlightenment unless truly internalized.

The only way to experience it is through practice. But if I've written my journey and readers still don't experience the same, perhaps it's because they learn the 'how' through words first and can't experience it as new.

It's like solving a riddle: for the thrill and satisfaction, one must not know the answer beforehand. If spoiled, the enjoyment fades.

This applies to stories too. Movies and shows are exciting because we don't know what's next. Hearing "The hero dies at the end" from a friend would ruin that fun with a spoiler.

Maybe that's why Esoteric Buddhism is secret. If methods to enlightenment are explained, they lose their effect on those who learn it without practice. Like a movie spoiler, it affects enjoyment.

Yet I admit this secrecy is a conjecture since we don't know the true nature of 'Secret Buddhism.'

Anyhow, as Zen riddles show, my philosophy that "everything is belief, so choose beneficial beliefs" may leave readers disadvantaged—unable to achieve enlightenment this way. **When knowledge or theories replace experiences, it can prevent realization, akin to trying to solve a riddle knowing the answer.** Not getting the answer to a riddle or spoilers for a movie can lessen experiences similarly.



## If Happiness Exists, We Can't Recognize It
The thing is, while caught up in theories and philosophy, happiness never comes. I'm stuck in the endless loop of thoughts, trapped in fear.

Maybe overcoming this requires realizing that life's explorations hold no truth, moving beyond these thought loops to find unknown happiness. If happiness exists, it doesn't come from these deep philosophical or religious traps but instead comes unaware. **Happiness feels like catching clouds.**

True happiness might be impossible to recognize. If we could, it might just be another illusion, perhaps happiness itself is an illusion. But, assuming it exists and is unrecognizable, it might become evident retrospectively. Happiness might be what we call a chain of experiences that make us think, "That was nice."

It's similar to sleeping. The moment of falling asleep isn't something we recognize. Do people who claim they can recognize that exist? You're aware of feeling cozy, drowsy, fading, but the actual moment of losing consciousness isn't catchable.

Happiness (if it exists) might be similar. True joy isn't realized in the moment, as you're engaged in it and forget about feeling happy. Only later can you feel, "That was fun!" Life is happy when these moments chain together. Focusing on striving for fun might mean it's not real joy.



## Enter the Temple? 
The current fear probably won't remain forever. It's unlikely, though possible, that this fear continues non-stop till I die. It's likely a belief. So, I might continue pondering but eventually get frustrated and move away from philosophical thinking, back to my usual daily life.

In a way, that could mean returning to a pre-enlightenment state, back when reaching enlightenment felt possible, but resulted in this (an endless loop of fear), showing that breaking free from suffering was just a belief as well.

So, I'll stop overthinking and instead believe what I want ("That’s not true!" for unwanted beliefs). Anger, suffering—consider them part of the universe's illusion, thus making them trivial. For joys and desires, conveniently use "common sense" to say, "That's not an illusion! That's real fun!" Returning to a pre-enlightenment state should be fine. Seeking enlightenment or freedom from all suffering doesn't matter anymore.

So, what now? Being a NEET gives me time for such deep thinking and delving into the void (I'm good at reaching nothingness). Let's start working again. Thinking work is painful might be an illusion. I had fun at times before. As someone with few social connections, workplace interactions are quite precious. I have an upcoming meeting with my agent. Let's start there.
