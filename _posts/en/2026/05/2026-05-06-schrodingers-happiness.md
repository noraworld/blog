## 'Cleanliness is Important' is a Misconception
In this article, I want to discuss the idea that "letting go of preconceptions, expectations, and logic, and trusting your intuition might lead to happiness." But before I talk about 'intuition' or 'happiness', let me explain how our preconceptions work.

Many women say they like men who have a sense of cleanliness. But I've met women who weren't exactly the clean type (sorry for saying something rude 🙇‍♂️, but I've never said it to anyone directly. Maybe not saying it is even harsher, but oh well...). After having some experiences, I started to think:

"These women always say 'cleanliness is important', but maybe they're not as aware of cleanliness themselves?"

But this is probably a misconception. First, what women mean by 'cleanliness' might not be the same as my definition of it. My idea of 'cleanliness' is things like "Are your nails trimmed?" "Do you brush your teeth properly?" or "Is there visible dandruff or lice in your hair?" But even if we agree on these three points, that doesn't mean my entire sense of cleanliness matches up with what most women mean.

Moreover, just because many women say "cleanliness is important" doesn't mean they all feel that way to the same extent. In fact, I might not have ever heard one of the "not-so-clean" women saying, "Guys value cleanliness, right?" in my life. Hardly ever have I directly heard a woman say "cleanliness is important" in real life, even though I see it on TV shows or videos about love.

When I think more about it, it might just be a trend. And for the "not-so-clean" woman I encountered—maybe "cleanliness isn't a big deal" for her. Of course, if we're talking about a binary choice between having or lacking cleanliness, people would probably prefer cleanliness. But that's the problem with framing the question that way. In reality, there might be many women who care about cleanliness as shallowly as one would about "liking people with genes that don't cause hangnails." When you phrase it like "Who is more attractive, someone who easily gets hangnails or someone who doesn't?" most people might lean toward the latter but care very little about it in truth.

So, the concept of "cleanliness is important" might not be as vital as a hangnail-level issue for some. Logically, if you think cleanliness is crucial, there might be an overwhelmingly large number of people who prefer it, but it might not be such a critical factor compared to traits like "kindness", "mindfulness", or for some, even "as long as he's good-looking, who cares about cleanliness." When put against those kinds of characteristics, cleanliness might not be that vital.

Further, there's also the possibility that people who say they "like handsome guys" but can't admit it due to the stigma of it being superficial might instead be attributing it to accessibly improvable traits like "cleanliness." They might care about looks, but talk up 'cleanliness', an aspect anyone could supposedly improve on with effort, instead of admitting to a fondness for certain genetics.

To assume that since many women say cleanliness is important, it applies to almost everyone—is a misconception; assuming they say so sincerely is another mistake, and even not questioning if there’s a gap in our definitions of 'cleanliness' is a misconception itself.

Earlier, I argued that in a binary sense—between having or lacking cleanliness, most people would choose having it. Even so, if adjusting that one variable—cleanliness—saves only a second of being late or causes expecting it from others, it could backfire or become a downside to some.

To live without any misconceptions in life is virtually impossible. If you were to eliminate every single one, you'd need to account for the possibility that a lion from the zoo could attack you right after stepping outside your door—making everyday life nerve-wracking.

Our brains are designed to save CPU resources by predicting that the next time is similar if we experience something many times. Therefore, after repeatedly hearing "Cleanliness is important to women," you might eventually get stuck on the idea of prioritizing grooming for a date—even without checking if the other person actually holds cleanliness paramount.

## 'Intuition' Lies on the Flip Side of Reason and Knows Happiness
So, in deciding actions, people should essentially "not overthink and act on intuition," deriving the somewhat unconventional conclusion that might seem unexpected for today’s rational age. No matter how much you focus on cleanliness—or even if you have verified that the other person truly believes so—it might just be them conforming to societal norms, hiding their preference for good looks over cleanliness. If you haven't been declared handsome by your date, pursuing a relationship might be unlikely.

However, saying "Cleanliness might not matter, so don’t care about it!" isn’t quite right either. Because clearly, the fact that “cleanliness is important” comes up as a topic at all matters. It’s not just one of those “I like people with genes that handle hangnails well” level gliding past unnoticed if it wasn’t of some importance.

So, it's not about obsessing over cleanliness or not caring at all about cleanliness—just avoiding calculative thinking. Don’t gauge “Will perfect grooming boost my date success rate?”—but neither disregard cleanliness. If you intuitively feel like checking yourself in the mirror before leaving home on a date, then go for it. If you think, “Meh, it’ll be fine,” then minimal focus is okay. Something like this suffices.

If the date already seems destined to fail, fixed into narrative already, trying hard won't change it—and perhaps your intuition quietly knew so, telling you not to bother too much with grooming.

And although rational people will prioritize reason and enjoy scientific progress, they shy from trusting intuition, worrying it’ll backfire and make them regret having trusted it.

However, much like with the example mentioned earlier, whereas reason may fail if things are set to fail, intuition might secretly give insights we consciously lack. If going out only feels tedious before a date, perhaps it’s because your intuition already knows its outcome. Feeling the hassle is intuition’s way of pointing something bigger.

The idea stands that a failed date could set the stage for future success, or pave a path for future dates—even if intuition knows this one will fail, saying so wouldn’t deter one from attending anyway to allow necessary growth.

Though it might fascinate you, rational people in the modern age have prioritized reason, enjoying scientific development’s benefits, making them hesitant to believe in intuition. If their attempts don’t pan out, they regret having trusted their gut.

If their pursuit is deemed a lost cause, intuition nudging them to not waste effort might make sense—rather than simply cutting corners—one might, however, remain hopeful that serendipity will come along.

The ironies abound. If intuition suggested failure’s inevitable, perhaps current missteps were sowing future seeds. Maybe said failure provided learnings for future success—this concept stands in stark contrast with rational, scientific approaches.

Now, in case mistakes still ensue, and today's pitfalls cause self-doubt down the road, recognizing which fears are unjustified (even when outcomes seem unfavorable) proves crucial. Pursue mutual respect, communal growth, and conscious connections, pursuing whole-hearted dreams, enjoying brighter days.
