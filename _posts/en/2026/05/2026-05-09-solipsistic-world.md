While reading '[Some Philosophical Thing, and Science, etc.](https://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/B07K8FHVBX)', I started wondering if this world is actually just me…? Could it all be just my imagination…? These thoughts brought on feelings of fear and loneliness.

## We Can't Rule Out the Possibility that All Dating Apps Are AI
AI has grown a lot, and sometimes it feels like it's responding just like a human—maybe even more human-like than real humans sometimes. In such a world, questioning "What is a human?" feels really philosophical and intriguing.

Take dating apps, for example. You look at photos, hit like, match, and exchange messages—all online. But what if it was all AI? Could you trust that?

You might think, "No way, that's not possible," but there's no way to verify it. Image-generating technology has improved, so you can create a believable profile photo. An AI can write an endless number of introduction paragraphs.

And when you swipe right on someone you find attractive and match, the conversation could be with an AI that mimics real conversation styles.

Of course, I'm not seriously claiming "Everyone on dating apps is AI." But theoretically, you can't completely rule out that possibility, especially if you're someone who uses advanced AI in daily life.

When chatting with ChatGPT, you know the responses are from an AI. But what if the interface suggested you were chatting with another human, and the responses were AI-generated? Some might not even notice. This is especially true for formal customer service chats or messages from people you've never met.

Given that, even if you were using a dating app and exchanged messages with someone, there's no way to completely rule out the idea that person might be AI. Even if the texts seem sloppy or have typos, or responses are inconsistent, that could all be simulated. You could write prompts like, "Make it messy" or, "Randomly insert some typos." Response timing could vary using random numbers, as could decisions about when or if to reply. Actual human-chat data could help create convincing response patterns.

You might argue, "But if you actually make plans to meet, it's a real human, right? It sounds far-fetched." That might be true 'right now' because AI doesn't have a physical "body."

But we have seen robots like Pepper. They look like robots, and it's clear they're not human. But what if manufacturing technology improved drastically? What if they looked completely human... ? If such an AI had an appointment to meet you and showed up—how could you be sure it was human?

You might think, "But humans have a brain that can accurately detect the nuances of a human's natural face and expressions, so duplicating a human look must be impossible, right? Especially with the uncanny valley effect." Certainly, it's technically difficult. But theoretically, it's not impossible. 

Before AI appeared, chatbot-like programs couldn't mimic human conversation perfectly. Most people thought they were convenient but obviously not human, and they failed to interpret inputs outside their intended role accurately.

Now, some AI can provide more accurate and natural conversations than humans. If you've seen this leap in technology, why insist it's impossible to mimic the human appearance? Considering the massive jump from early AI concepts to what we have now, it’s not unimaginable that someday there will be robots that look almost human and are powered by AI.

In short, fundamentally, you can't truly determine if someone is human. Right now, because super-realistic robots don't exist, meeting in person helps, or thinking everyone on a dating app is a human seems reasonable and ethically/legal, you can believe the other person is human. But as technology advances, distinguishing between humans and AI will gradually blur.



## Is This World Just a Dream?
There's a popular game called [Detroit: Become Human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detroit:_Become_Human). Many of you might know about it. In this story, androids that look like humans coexist or rebel with humanity, dividing human and android fates. The game starts with the line "This is your future," hinting that such a future might come, especially with the progress and penetration of AI.

As you progress in the game, you get surveyed with questions like, "Do you believe androids have a heart?" When I played it, I thought, "Their so-called heart is just what's programmed, so androids can't have a heart."

But after reading '[Some Philosophical Thing, and Science, etc.](https://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/B07K8FHVBX)', my view changed. Not to "androids will eventually develop real emotions," but rather, "we've never confirmed if other humans even have emotions either."

It may sound insane, but the belief "I have feelings, so others must too" could be a delusion. You might think, "But I’ve seen people feel happy or angry just like me," yet you've never confirmed this. It could be robots acting with fake emotions, or brains simulated to feel emotions that we can't dismiss.

Thinking "I have emotions, so isn't it strange not to extend this to others...?" doesn't mean your feelings aren't just an illusion your brain crafted. What if only you have feelings, and the universe is just your brain's creation with humans imitating emotion-having creatures? Just playing a role-playing game in such a world is theoretically possible, even if we can't verify if others have emotions or even if you're dreaming the cosmos.

If you're still skeptical: imagine you determine you're seeing a vast cosmic dream, go to the hospital, check your brain, and confirm you're fine. How do you prove "going to the hospital to be checked" wasn't a dream itself? Can you realize in a dream that it's a dream? If you've experienced that (lucid dreaming), saying "That was the dream"—how do you argue it's not another layer of dreaming?

## Scientific Ramblings
This is more of a theory or possibility, which might sound like nonsense. You might argue, "What good comes from thinking about such unscientific stuff?"

But even in physics, a similar situation arises. It's called 'Schrödinger's Cat.' While famous, its details can be surprisingly hard to grasp, so I'll leave the in-depth explanation to '[Some Philosophical Thing, and Science, etc.](https://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/B07K8FHVBX).' The gist is "unless it's observed, it's only a hypothesis or interpretation."

That's why physics (quantum mechanics) doesn't dismiss "this world might be parallel worlds." If an electron might exist at point A or B until measured, then a cat in a box might be alive or dead until checked. So humans seeing a live or dead cat might exist in parallel worlds.…

'Parallel worlds' may be an interpretation, not confirmed. There’s 'Pilot-wave theory'—another interpretation that’s consistent. But in the absence of observing it (testing or evidence), you can only say it's possible or not possible (current physics uses the Copenhagen interpretation for practicality, not confirming its truth; it's called an 'interpretation' not a 'theory').

If this universe is parallel worlds, if what I'm vividly feeling might be a dream... ......
