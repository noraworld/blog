## Introduction
I want to clarify right off the bat that I personally do go to vote. Although I've only started recently, I still can't agree with the opinion that "not voting is questionable." The simple reason being, if we recognize the freedom to choose which party to vote for, then we should also recognize the freedom not to vote.

This discussion is based on the current system in Japan and doesn't consider countries like [Australia and Belgium, where there are penalties for not voting](https://www.city.sakata.lg.jp/kurashi/senkyo/school.files/w-gimu.pdf).

## 'Curry-flavored Poop' and 'Poop-flavored Curry'
Often people say, "If you don't vote, you can't complain about the results." This argument seems off to me. It's like asking, "**Which would you pick: curry-flavored poop or poop-flavored curry?**" and when someone says, "I wouldn't choose either," responding with, "**Then don't complain when someone else makes you eat one of them**."

For someone who thinks all the options are like poop, forcing them to pick between 'curry-flavored poop' and 'poop-flavored curry' and making them spend time and effort attending the election seems unreasonable.

While thought experiments like choosing between 'curry-flavored poop' and 'poop-flavored curry' can be intriguing, forcing every voter to answer them (and criticizing those who don't vote) is absurd. You don't have the right to criticize someone for not wanting to answer that question.

I’m not saying that the current politics are as terrible as that choice. Also, I’m not mocking you for going out to vote between 'curry-flavored poop' and 'poop-flavored curry'. But some people see all options as poop, and disregarding their views is no different from insulting people who support specific parties.

## Not Allowing the Freedom Not to Vote Is Justified Bullying
In Japan, talks about politics or religion are often considered taboo because they can lead to arguments. Given this cultural tendency, if someone sees others voting for a party with opposing ideologies, they might want to criticize internally, but few will express it openly to avoid damaging relationships.

Perhaps, people have this bottled-up urge to criticize something, and their target ends up being "those who don’t vote." Criticizing them is easy. We've all seen interviews where non-voters say their reason is that it’s "bothersome" or "nothing changes anyway."

But isn't that manipulation by (old) media? Why assume all non-voters think the same? Doesn't "I have no party to vote for" sound like a valid reason? Yet, those claiming it’s "bothersome" are easily labeled as "lazy individuals who abandon their voting rights," providing an easy target for people who avoid confrontations about opposing political views. It's like **justified bullying**.

Supporting opposing politicians should be accepted in a democracy because choosing which party to vote for is personal freedom. Why, then, is the **freedom not to vote** not recognized?

## The Public Voice: "Low Voter Turnout"
While I understand concerns that low voter turnout means "public opinion isn't reflected" or "special interest votes become influential," arguing non-voting is like saying, "Voting for this party means you're a fool." It's personal ego affecting election results. Not voting might strengthen specific party forces, but isn’t this too a form of "public opinion"?

Luckily, there are no penalties for not voting in Japan. So, not voting for a while won't strip you of the right to vote (for now). Basically, you can decide to go anytime.

Actually, that itself reflects the public's voice. When the ruling party makes a huge blunder or directly impacts personal lives, the voter turnout increases. Overconfidence from the ruling party may lead to losing their grip, occasionally.

A low turnout means immense potential for opposing parties, reflecting public opinion. Politicians aren't dumb; they know this. And it's a deterrent. Hence, non-voting doesn’t mean public opinion isn’t being reflected.

Forcing everyone to vote, including those without political interest or understanding, can lead to bizarre outcomes. History (and a bit of philosophy) shows this. Populism, where clueless voters cast votes based merely on appearances, poses a risk.

Isn't this worrisome? Rather than uniform high voter turnout, where parties desperately cling to supporters, risking instability and reduced flexibility, fluctuating turnout according to political climates seems more natural and reflects the true public sentiment.

If everyone voted all the time, politicians might exploit this stability and push favorable policies. A lower turnout with unpredictable outcomes forces politicians to be cautious, thus turning the tables on them. This too is a form of public opinion.

### High or Low Turnout: Both Reflect Public Opinion
You might argue I'm just musing possibilities given my frequent use of the word "possibility." You're right. I'm no politics expert, this is just an amateur's rant. But think about it: is there any solid proof that higher turnout means public opinion is better reflected? Isn't that your imagination too?

Countries with compulsory voting exist, but applying these systems wholesale in Japan is no guarantee. With various factors like culture, temperament, values, and other variables, both sides remain hypothetical.

So let's be clear: I'm not saying "uninformed people shouldn’t vote." We're discussing possibilities, not absolutes, and challenging the opinion that getting everyone to vote is automatically better for public opinion.

## The Ego of "Not Voting Is Bad"
Thus, my main point is that labeling non-voting as bad and criticizing it is **imposing one's justice on others.** Ancient Greece had democracy but with its issues, transcended through time. We venerate democracy today, forgetting lessons already learned centuries ago.

Of course, modern democracy isn't identical to the ancient variant. But it's still evolving, with problems unsolved since Aristotle's time. Criticizing non-voters under the guise of "increasing turnout reflects public opinion" may overlook crucial historical insights.

Finally, to prevent misunderstanding: I'm not criticizing democracy; I vote and accept that even the politically uninformed should have the right to vote in a democracy.

I wrote this to awaken those reveling in the righteousness of criticizing non-voters. Understanding "there are different viewpoints" towards non-voters can widen perspectives and allow for more flexible thinking.
