_Live in the 'now'._

### The Future is Unpredictable

I (or humans in general) have a tendency to fear or worry about the future, but while learning about Eastern philosophy and Buddhism, I realized that worrying about things decades ahead is just a waste of time and effort. Let me try to articulate this logically in my own words.

First, let's define "old age" as a time decades (or more) from now. Our modern society changes too quickly, so it's hard to even predict a few years ahead. How many people in the year 2000 could foresee the emergence of smartphones (iPhones) in 2008, a groundbreaking device that everyone now owns? Similarly, in 2020, how many people predicted that AI would grow so rapidly and become a normal part of many lives? The term AI existed before, but how it would become widespread and change people's lives was something most couldn't predict.

So there’s no way anyone can guess what will happen decades into the future. Perhaps geniuses who are incredibly skilled at forecasting can roughly predict up to ten years, but even that is not certain. How can ordinary people, or even geniuses, predict a future decades ahead that most people cannot foresee? If I may be so bold, "Even geniuses can't predict the future decades from now, so don't be so arrogant as to think you can."



### The Myth of Security

Confident because of pensions? There was a time when pensions disappeared in Japan. Confident because of savings? If the yen keeps devaluing, their worth diminishes, and if global prices rise, you might not support yourself in the future, even if you're okay now.

Confident because of investments and asset management? What if decades later communism makes a sudden surge, making your capitalist assets worthless? When Russia became the Soviet Union, what happened to the wealthy capitalists then? Could they have imagined during the Russian Revolution that their assets would be seized by the state? If it can't happen, why are communist ideas spreading in the capitalist hub of the USA?

At a fundamental level, no matter how secure things seem now, you might step outside tomorrow and get hit by a drunk driver and die. So what would you do? Never go out to stay safe?

If you really want to avoid all fears and dangers, you'd never step outside since it's risky, switch jobs if you need to commute and go full remote, always order Uber Eats to avoid outside risks even if it's pricier, move abroad because Japan has earthquakes, learn new languages, avoid spending money on hobbies because you might get laid off... The list goes on.

Does it seem silly? Well, I think worrying about old age is just a further extension of this. Fears only bring stress and are pointless. While many deem fears necessary, their measures are usually half-hearted. People go out without any urgent need, ride roller coasters that are more accident-prone than airplanes, surf despite the jellyfish sting risk, eat raw fish considering the anisakis risk, have intimate relations despite infection risks... It's all contradictory.

I'm not here to senselessly instill fear. The point is, while we can think endlessly about every possible future fear, it’s useless. Humans have brains evolved to think about the future for better survival chances, fueled by the negative emotion of worry. The brain is **a worry-producing machine** unrivaled when it comes to generating fears.

But that worked well in the primal days, where survival of the fittest ruled amidst the slow-changing environment. Modern society changes too fast, and our actions to avoid future fears mostly don't pan out. Plus, if those fears paralyze you, preventing you from focusing on the present, it's meaningless too.



### The Youngest Moment of the Rest of Your Life is Right Now

You're now living in the youngest moment you’ll have for the rest of your life. Older people often say that time in youth is precious. Aging diminishes your stamina and enthusiasm, and some experiences are tough to have once you're older.

So, **using the most valuable time in the rest of your life to worry and fret about old age isn't logical and is a waste of time.** Instead, you should fully immerse yourself in what you've got to do or want to do now.

Never think that contemplating the future makes you logical, wise, stable, or sensible while thinking those who don't are foolish. That's the trap of emotional deception crafted by primal brains as the gateway to hell. It plays into your brain's hands, which exists to keep you alive but offers no fulfillment or happiness.



### Throwing Away 97% of Possibilities for 3%

Some reading this may wish to argue. They find it unfair that carefree people get rewarded while those serious about their future suffer. Our brains are inherently wired to handle negativity and act accordingly, so this opposes our instincts. Or, society has taught us that a stable, cautious life is ideal through ~~indoctrination~~ education.

But think again. Even if all preventive measures make efforts futile without full future foresight and even if successful, ask yourself if you truly want a future where continuous worry rides alongside you for decades? After wrestling with thoughts of old age for decades, acting on those fears and surviving, could you ever say, "Good job, I'm happy now?" Can you enjoy carefreeness and resent those who don't, expecting karma to strike and finding happiness in that?

This isn't about quitting efforts. It's about redirecting them. Studying hard and aiming for good universities should be about fulfilling dreams, not securing stable jobs that promise safety (especially in roles you don't care for). Misplaced goals bring void efforts, and the void obliterates any sense of security, even if outwardly stable.

Sacrificing your true desires only to prepare for old age guarantees future regrets. When nearing the end, you'll wish you'd done things differently. With the chance of dying tomorrow not zero, would you rather worry over distant futures and limit yourself, or pursue a life fully engaged in the now, with no regrets no matter when it ends?

In addition, **97% of human worries don’t happen or seem trivial**, according to studies. It's for you to choose between a life bogged down by future anxiety, wiping out present opportunities, and a life acknowledging 3% inevitable unpleasantness yet living joyfully in the moment.

_Live in the 'now'._



### The Recommendation of Not Overthinking

Though it might seem harsh thus far, I’ll suggest a practical approach to ease it out. Humans naturally blame their past or dread the future. Fighting these emotions is unfortunately impossible.

But keeping such thinking minimal allows discomfort its rightful place in your life. **Stop thinking**—more precisely, once you notice negative emotions, recognize them, calmly resume what you're doing, and keep repeating this. Before trying, you might doubt its effectiveness in easing burdens, but upon attempting, your heart lightens more than expected.

Repeating once more; this won’t solve every problem, nor eliminate negativity fully. Aiming for that could lead to suffering. However, it reduces primitive mind control significantly.

If dissatisfied with my words, here’s a gentle, encouraging book recommendation. Its essence aligns with my words here, but if you disagree, give it a read for more insight.

[The Practice of Not Overthinking](https://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/B0CQLXLM5M)



### Afterword

I apologize for such a strong title like "Worrying About Old Age is Foolish." I once acted foolish too. But impactful words update values more easily, and that shock helped soothe my mind.

Reflecting back, my life always struggled amidst worries, never arriving at resolution through confrontation and overthinking, hitting cognitive overload and liberating me to act freely ended up being the key. With time, forgetting these success stories brought relapse into negativity—a continuous cycle with sparse bouts of 'invincible mode.'

However, now having mastered this mindset and applied it consciously to daily life, I'm shocked by being able to form **an unfounded confidence of "Everything will be okay, as long as I keep practicing this mindset"**, altering me significantly with just one thought change.

I ponder why I opted for such a soft finish eventually—perhaps trying to avoid "criticism," aligning with trying to end it mildly, signifying I still have room to grow.
