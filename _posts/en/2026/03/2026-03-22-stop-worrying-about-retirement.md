_Live in the 'now.'_

### The Future Can't Be Predicted
I (and humanity in general) have a tendency to feel strong fear or anxiety about the future. But after learning more about Eastern philosophy and Buddhism, I realized that worrying about things decades from now or about retirement is a waste of time and energy. So I thought I'd try to articulate this in my own words, as logically as possible.

First, let's define old age as several decades ahead from now (or more). Our modern society changes too fast; it's hard to predict even a few years ahead. How many people in 2000 could have predicted that a groundbreaking device like the smartphone (iPhone) would come out in 2008? In 2020, how many could have predicted that AI would grow so rapidly and be used widely by many? Even though the term AI existed earlier, few could predict how it would permeate society and change people's lives.

We certainly can't know what the future several decades from now will look like. Maybe a genius who specializes in future forecasting can predict up to ten years ahead, but even that isn't guaranteed. So why would most people, who aren't geniuses, believe they can predict a future that even geniuses can't? To put it a bit harshly: "**Who are you to think you can predict a future decades from now that even a genius can't?**"


### The Myth of Security
Having a pension ensures security? Japan has a history of pensions disappearing. Saving on your own means being secure? If inflation continues and prices rise worldwide, saving might not be enough to cover living costs in a few decades.

Invest in assets and you'll be secure? Maybe in decades to come, communism will rise and make capitalist assets worthless. During the transition from the Russian Empire to the USSR, did the wealthy capitalists foresee their assets being seized by the state? Probably not. If you're sure that won't happen, why is communist thought gaining traction in capitalist America?

Here's a more straightforward example: Even if everything seems stable now, what if you get in an accident tomorrow, hit by a car from a drunk driver? Would you stay home forever to avoid risk?

The list of what-ifs is endless. If you wanted to avoid all risks, never going outside would be the best strategy. Choosing jobs that allow remote work, using services like Uber Eats to avoid going out, moving abroad to escape earthquakes in Japan, learning foreign languages for this potential relocation, not spending on hobbies fearing you might lose your job... 

Sounds ridiculous, doesn't it? But worrying about retirement is just another form of this pattern. Anxiety only adds stress. If we truly believed in avoiding uncertainty, our safety measures would be more thorough. Yet people go out unnecessarilly, ride roller coasters despite higher accident rates, surf despite jellyfish, eat sushi despite Anisakis risks, and engage in close contact with loved ones despite the risk. It's all contradictory.

I'm not here to stir up unnecessary fear. My point is that concerns about the future are endless and ultimately useless. Human brains have evolved to think about the future to increase survival rates and generate anxiety to push us to act. Essentially, **the human brain is an anxiety machine.** On this front, it's unmatched.

Yet, this mechanism worked well in primitive times when life was survival of the fittest and society wasn't changing rapidly. Today, society changes so fast that anxiety and actions to avoid it often don't lead to desired outcomes. Being overwhelmed by this fear and neglecting present tasks is counterproductive.


### The Youngest Moment in Your Life is Now
The youngest you'll ever be is right now. The elderly all agree that youth is precious. As you age, both your energy and will decline, making some experiences difficult.

Wasting the most valuable time of your life worrying about the future isn't logical; it’s a waste. Instead, focus all your energy on what you should and want to do now.

Don't think that considering the future carefully makes you logical, smart, or prudent, nor that living in the present makes someone foolish. That's a trap set by primitive brain patterns and the beginning of hell. It's falling into the brain's trap that perpetuates suffering. The primitive brain only exists to keep you alive, not to provide fulfillment or happiness.


### A Life that Sacrifices 97% for 3%
Some reading this might want to argue. They believe living carefree is advantageous while those who consider the future and work earnestly get left behind, which seems unfair. It's hard to break from a brain wired for negativity because acting against instincts is challenging. Plus, society tells us that planning for the future leads to success and stable, happy lives.

But think carefully once more. Can you honestly say you'd enjoy a life spent anxious about a future you can't predict, despite precautions? Even if you outsmart future risks, would living in constant fear be the life you're proud of? If carefree individuals suffer, does it bring you happiness?

Giving up isn't the answer. It's about ensuring efforts aren't misdirected. Studying hard for a great college should be about fulfilling dreams like becoming a doctor to save lives, not merely guaranteeing future stability. Mistaking the purpose of efforts leads to a void and unresolved anxiety, even if from the outside things seem stable.

Similarly, sacrificing your current desires for retirement concerns ensures regret eventually. Near life's end, those ignored desires might become serious regrets. Would you prefer living under constraints from distant future worries, or living life fully, content whether life ends tomorrow? That choice is yours alone.

To add, **97% of human anxieties never happen, or aren't as bad as imagined.** Sacrificing precious moments now for speculative worries or living joyfully despite the 3% odds of unpleasant events—that choice too is yours.

_Live in the 'now.'_


### The Case for Less Thinking
This might seem harsh, so here’s a brief practical approach. Humans naturally self-blame for past failures and fear future uncertainties. Eliminating such thoughts entirely isn't possible.

However, minimizing suffering from these emotions is achievable by **stopping the over-thinking.** More precisely, notice negative thoughts, calm down, and return to the present task. Repeat this cycle. While it might seem ineffective before trying, practice surprisingly lightens the burden.

Of course, you won't eliminate all worries or negative emotions entirely, and trying only causes more pain. However, at least it’s easier than letting primal brain patterns control you completely.

### Afterword
Apologies for the harsh "Worrying About Retirement Is Pointless" statement. I was once naive. But impactful words can facilitate valuable perspective updates, as was the case for myself.

Reflecting, during anxious times, my life went poorly. Yet, solutions never emerged from deep analysis; change only occurred post mental overcapacity, upon embracing what I truly wanted. With success fleetingly from this "invincible mode,” only to regress to negativity seemed an eternal cycle.

Now, sincerely adopting these ideas daily prompted a **"baseless confidence"** of assuredness against any future adversities, solely from shifting thought. I'm astounded by the inner transformation from just a mental change.

Unexpectedly, concluding gently might seem paradoxical, driven perhaps by nervous anticipation of criticism. Consequently, I too remain a student in this endeavor.
