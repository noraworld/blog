### The Curse and Struggles of Perfectionism
I have a tendency towards perfectionism.

People's opinions are divided when they hear about this personality trait. When I was in high school or around that time, I once vented on Twitter about how tough perfectionism was. Someone replied, "Isn't it amazing to be able to do things perfectly?" While they probably didn't mean any harm, that reply made me feel misunderstood.

As I mentioned in that episode, perfectionism is a negative personality trait for me, at least. If it worked well, that would be fine. But most tasks and situations in life aren't simple enough to be done perfectly. It can take all day just to figure out what needs to be done for perfection.

And the standards for what's perfect are vague. How far do you need to go to achieve "perfection"? Perfectionists often don't set clear standards, leading to endless efforts. You start thinking, "Shouldn't I do this as well? Maybe I need to do that too," and soon, there's no end in sight. It's like running a marathon without a finish line.

The hardest part is that trying to do things perfectly is so overwhelming and time-consuming that just getting started becomes a chore. I can't even bring myself to start anything, and everything feels like a hassle. There are things I want to do, things I need to do—tasks piling up in front of me—but I can't tackle any of them... This struggle can often lead to depression.

Besides, often the results don't change much even if things aren't perfect. In fact, by deciding "this is good enough" and diving in, things often go faster and end more quickly. The result can be more satisfying to me in the end. Essentially, perfectionism is self-satisfaction, and a perfect effort doesn't guarantee a better outcome.

I've struggled with this trait for a long time, and it hasn't changed. Even when I understand in my head that I should stop trying to do everything perfectly, I still find reasons to strive for perfection. It's a cognitive habit, like a routine, and it's not easy to change quickly.

### The Magic Words "Oh Well"
Recently, however, I've discovered a way of thinking that somewhat eases the hardness of perfectionism: "Oh well."

Words are curious things; when you say them out loud, they start to feel true. If saying it is too hard, just thinking the words can help.

If you want to make it stronger, try adding, "It doesn’t matter because..." For example, with cleaning. When you start noticing dirt, you find one messy spot after another, and trying to clean everything turns into a big project like year-end cleaning (though I haven’t done that alone because it sounds tedious). In such cases, say, "Oh well, it doesn’t matter because it’ll just get dirty again even if I clean it perfectly now." Saying or thinking that can make you feel lighter and free you from the curse of perfectionism, allowing you to accept that it's okay not to be perfect.

This works for brushing teeth too. I have a cavity that needs fixing, and I’ve been trying to brush carefully to slow its progression, once brushing for up to 30 minutes. I'm a bit better now, but 15 minutes is still common, and honestly, it's tiring.

But honestly, brushing the same spot repeatedly isn't necessary, and to really prevent cavities, seeing a dentist regularly in addition to brushing at home is ideal. So I know in my head that spending so much time brushing is overdoing it.

Yet my perfectionist side won’t allow it. "What if you brush less and feel a toothache the next day? It’d be because you spent less time brushing," these excuses stop me from reducing the time. Even though I know I'm almost brushing perfectly at home, the "Oh well" can help here too.

### Letting Go of Perfectionism is Like Working Out
Of course, this isn't a magic cure for perfectionism. Even now, I sometimes fall into perfectionist thinking. For example, when writing something that's going to be seen by others, I can think that there shouldn’t be any typographical errors or inconsistencies, and that things need to be neatly structured with perfect chapter divisions. Sometimes, this stops me from writing anything at all, or I forget what I was trying to write. At worst, I start thinking I must be perfect in every way, and this keeps me from writing.

But I see this as a form of training. We all know it’s impossible to become a bodybuilder overnight. Similarly, each effort might not be so strong, but turning this way of thinking into a habit might gradually free me from the grip of perfectionism. I’m still on this path myself.

Scientifically, perfectionism doesn't seem to offer many benefits and is more noted for its drawbacks, so overcoming it is often advised. Mark Zuckerberg, who created Facebook, also said, "Done is better than perfect." When someone who built such a huge service says that, it must hold truth. Producing a few perfect things is less valuable than creating a hundred good enough things. Whether it's a service, a game, or a book, you never really know what'll be a hit.

### Finally
If you see yourself as a perfectionist and it's leading to a successful life without complaints or stress, that's wonderful. But for those struggling due to perfectionism like I am, I hope this can be of some help.
