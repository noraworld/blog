### Introduction
Before writing this, I wanted to write an article about trying out note more actively, but yesterday (2/28) I wasn't feeling great, so I couldn't. The order of publishing might get mixed up, but with my memories and impressions still fresh, I'm prioritizing this one.

This morning (3/1), right after waking up and looking out the window, I suddenly thought, "Yes, I'll buy an Apple Watch," and immediately headed to the Apple Store in Kawasaki to buy one. After purchasing, I went straight home, set it up, and started writing this article. I bought the Apple Watch Ultra 3 (with a Trail Loop Black S/M band).

![Apple Bag](https://noraworld.github.io/box-venusaur/2026/03/01/6d6505388859e289c2b0f5eebde4a992.jpg)

### Why I Bought It
It might sound like an impulse buy, but it wasn't. I've been wanting a new Apple Watch for a while. I've been using an Apple Watch for a long time, but until today, I was still using the Series 4. When I looked up the purchase date, it was December 24, 2018, at 17:59. I've been using it continuously since then, for over 7 years.

I use it not just as a watch but for monitoring my heart rate, recording exercise, and tracking my sleep for health purposes. I wear it almost all the time since the end of 2018, except when I charge it during my showers.

However, the battery doesn't last a whole day, so I also had to charge it other times. Especially when I used it to track sleep, even if I charged it fully beforehand, it would drop to 50-60% by the time I woke up, making it hard to last until my evening shower.

I've been using it for over 7 years without replacing the battery, so it's pretty worn out. Lately, it started having clear problems, like shutting down immediately after a full charge and showing 90% upon reboot but quickly dropping to 10%.

Going out was stressful; if I forgot to charge it, it wouldn't last until I got home. Plus, I wore it overnight, so I couldn't leave right after waking up. I measure my walks with the Apple Watch when going out, and even a half-day out would leave it at 30% when I got back.

I couldn't keep using it like this, so I wanted a new Apple Watch. Plus, newer models can track data like blood oxygen that the Series 4 can't, and I love collecting data.

I hadn't decided when to buy it specifically, but this morning, I just felt like going to get it, so I did.

### Timing the Purchase
I tend to make decisions like this based on significant dates, so the decision to buy it today was because it was the 1st (the beginning of March). I've had financial struggles for a while, but I finally paid off my 2025 taxes and fees at the end of January, so I had a bit of financial leeway after my January payout, which came in the day before yesterday. So, I felt today was a good time to buy it.

My concern was that a new series might get released right after I bought it. I don't usually jump on new releases (I've used my last Apple Watch for over 7 years, and my current iPhone for about 4-5 years), but if new features were only in a new series, I'd feel like I missed out. Apple products are known for holding their value, so I prefer getting the latest if I'm paying a lot.

But if I keep waiting for the right time, new models will keep coming out, and I'll just miss my chance, so I needed to take the plunge.

The Apple Watch Ultra 3 was released last fall, and previous versions like the 2 and the original also came out in the fall, with a 2-year gap between 2 and 3. Based on this trend, Ultra 4 might come out this fall at the earliest. I've wanted a new Apple Watch long before now, so waiting until fall wasn't an option. Apple sometimes releases minor updates in spring (March), but that's unlikely for the Ultra series, so I figured this timing was fine.

### Why I Chose Ultra
As you probably guessed from earlier, I chose the Ultra because its battery lasts the longest.

Currently, the latest Apple Watch models are Series 11, SE 3, and Ultra 3. As you can see on the [comparison page](https://www.apple.com/jp/watch/compare/), Ultra 3 has the longest battery life. Series 11 lasts up to 24 hours, SE 3 lasts 18 hours, and Ultra 3 lasts 42 hours.

Back in 2018, my main criterion was a lightweight model that wouldn't get in the way. I remember there wasn't a model back then that lasted over 24 hours, and I still think my criteria were perfect for that time. But after using it for over 7 years, battery life became my main gripe, so I switched it up to "Whichever lasts the longest."

Series 4 had aluminum and stainless options, and I remember thinking the stainless looked more stylish, but I chose aluminum since it was lighter and less obtrusive. There were also two sizes, 44mm and 40mm, and I went for the smaller one for the same reason. Both choices ended up being the cheapest Series 4 model. It was costly back then at 61,344 yen.

Conversely, the Ultra 3 is the complete opposite: it's big. It comes only in 49mm, even larger than the biggest Series 11. The material is exclusively titanium. The Series 11 comes in both aluminum and titanium options, but the aluminum is surely lighter. I didn't try the Series 11 since I wasn't buying it, but I should have as a reference. While I haven't seen the exact grams listed by Apple, Ultra 3 is likely the heaviest and bulkiest among current models.

And price-wise, Ultra 3 is the most expensive. Depending on your choice of band and Series 11 or SE 3 options, some models cost under 100,000 yen, while Ultra 3 starts at around 130,000 yen. It's expensive... double the price of the Series 4. But it's a high-end model, and exchange rates play a part. It was a painful expenditure, but since I wear it almost constantly, it was something I was bound to buy sooner or later. With it lasting long, skimping would only lead to regret, so I decided to splurge.

### Watch Band
I had four bands to choose from: Alpine Loop, Trail Loop, Ocean Band, and Titanium Milanese Loop. As soon as I entered the store, a beautiful assistant helped me try on all the bands for about two hours. Despite being a busy Sunday, she assisted me almost entirely one-on-one 🙏.

Ultimately, I went with the Trail Loop as it was the least obtrusive. Since a band's performance doesn't matter, I went with the same criteria as my Series 4: lightweight and unobtrusive.

Honestly, the Ocean Band was my favorite in terms of looks. If I were choosing based on appearance, I'd have picked it. But when prioritizing usability over aesthetics, I thought I'd get more bothered by something cumbersome than how it looks, so I went for usability.

Not only was it practical, but the Trail Loop was the most comfortable and easiest to put on and take off. While the Alpine Loop was simple, too, the absence of metal parts made the Trail Loop lighter and less obtrusive.

The Ocean Band and Titanium Milanese Loop demanded a bit of finesse during use. I didn't quite master putting them on, so the assistant mostly did it for me.

The first one I ruled out was the Titanium Milanese Loop. It's just a personal bias, but it felt like a design for 'cool' middle-aged men, which didn't suit me. I'm pretty sure there was a similar band back in 2018, and I felt the same then. Plus, it's quite heavy and uncomfortable. Keeping a chunk of metal on my wrist felt odd, and it was 16,000 yen pricier when bought as a set. None of it suited me, so I ruled it out first. I heard it's around 30,000 yen on its own... expensive.

Next, I weighed the Alpine Loop and Trail Loop against the Ocean Band. This was a battle between usability/comfort and aesthetics. Inside the store, surrounded by people, I unconsciously valued appearance more. But I hardly go out, and I've never worn a watch as a fashion piece. I started wearing one only with the Apple Watch for health and data tracking, so I knew appearance wouldn't matter. But in the store's chic environment, I strangely felt a stronger urge to wear something stylish—kind of scary when you think about it.

Logically, functionality wins over appearance. Plus, you can buy additional bands and switch them later. When I asked, the assistant said the Alpine and Trail Loops were 15,000 yen each, while the Ocean Band was around 6,800 yen. However, if bought as a set, they all cost the same (except the Titanium Milanese Loop). If I ever decided to prioritize appearance, it would be cheaper to get the Ocean Band later. So, I figured this time, I'd choose between the Alpine or Trail Loop.

In the final decision between the Alpine and Trail Loops, each was much easier to put on and more suited for everyday use compared to the Ocean Band or Titanium Milanese Loop. While both had cheaper-looking fabric material, with usability in mind, the Trail Loop was the clear winner.

While the Alpine Loop did have a metal hook and a striped design adding a touch of style (not as chic as the Ocean Band), practicality was more important to me, so I picked the Trail Loop. That choice wasn't really difficult. It was more challenging resisting the allure of the Ocean Band's design.

For colors, the Trail Loop offered Blue, Green, and Black. The bright Green wasn't my style, so I ruled it out. When I told the assistant, "Bright colors like this don't suit me," she responded with a polite, "Oh, really?" but her forced politeness was apparent, and I felt a slight "ugh" (it wasn't offensive, though).

As for Blue, the Ocean Band had a subdued dark blue, while the Trail Loop's blue tone seemed too flashy. So, within the Trail Loop options, I went for Black.

The Trail Loop came in two sizes: S/M and M/L. The M/L was out of stock at the Kawasaki store, but my arm is pretty slim, so S/M was fine. The assistant said I was delicate ("kasha" is a great vocabulary choice, showing her high communication skills). Even at the tightest setting, there was still room, so I went with the available S/M. M/L would have fit too, so no size worries.

### Watch Body Color
The Ultra 3's material is titanium, and the size is fixed at 49mm, but the color is selectable. You can choose between Natural and Black. Natural resembles a mix of gray and silver. Both have a matte finish, adding a touch of luxury.

Since you can't change the body color like bands, careful selection is necessary. But it was only about color, so I wasn't overly stressed about it.

The body color mattered in relation to the band, not by itself. To choose, I considered the band I was buying (the Trail Loop) and the Ocean Band that I adored for its style.

Generally, I prefer dark colors. My Series 4 had a navy band. Since there's a blackish band available for each band type, matching the body to Black offers uniformity.

But I felt an accent between dark bands and a slightly brighter body color (it's not really bright, just brighter compared to black, still subtle and chic) would achieve visual harmony. Especially since I only wear black shirts. You need to try them on in-store to see this for yourself, so if you're considering an Apple Watch purchase, I recommend giving it a try.

For both the Trail Loop I bought and the stylish Ocean Band, pairing colors like Black or Blue seemed to go better with Natural, so I chose it.

### Purchasing Process
I completed the purchase and received it in-store. I could pay with cash, a card, iD, or QUICPay. I initially thought only cash or card was possible, so I brought my card, but finding out QUICPay worked too, I used it. I've faced financial hardships over the years with frequent late fees and collection fees due to missed credit card payments and the lag in billing causing stress. I'm progressing with a "No Credit Card Plan," primarily using a debit card, so paying 130,000 yen in one go was immediate (my debit card's linked to QUICPay). I'll write more about this plan in another article.

I opted not to get Apple Care because, in over 7 years with the Series 4, I never needed it. While repair costs of 80,000 yen without it sounded extreme, even with Apple Care, there's a 10,000 yen service fee, and I'm avoiding subscriptions whenever possible, so I skipped it.

### Setup
You can unbox and set up in-store if you prefer. While you can ask for help, it's mostly self-directed. Apple's setup is super simple, so I didn't expect problems. Plus, transferring everything from the Series 4 is best done at home. That's why I took it home for setup.

When unpairing from the old Apple Watch, remove any transit IC cards like Suica first, or it becomes a hassle later (it gets unpaired automatically but not transferred, leading to a hassle). The store assistant informed me, and it's something I vaguely knew. The following articles explain this in detail. I wish Apple's support page emphasized this more and changed the auto policy!

- [Transferring Apple Watch｜neko-yu](https://note.com/neco_u_/n/nf7b8d3173680)
- [Unpairing and removing Apple Watch - Apple Support (Japan)](https://support.apple.com/ja-jp/108372)
- [Unpairing and removing Apple Watch - Apple Support (Japan)](https://support.apple.com/ja-jp/guide/watch/apd4ad3571d9/watchos)
- [Setting up and pairing Apple Watch with iPhone - Apple Support (Japan)](https://support.apple.com/ja-jp/guide/watch/apdde4d6f98e/watchos)

I wondered if I'd need to transfer data from the old Apple Watch, but apparently, the iPhone syncs automatically, keeping constant backups when nearby. So, after moving my Suica card, unpairing was no issue. This seamlessness is a nice Apple feature.

[Backing up Apple Watch - Apple Support (Japan)](https://support.apple.com/ja-jp/108358)

Unpairing the old and pairing the new Apple Watch went smoothly. However, the old Apple Watch's battery issue flared when it dropped to 0% during the process, despite starting at 70%. Fortunately, the unpairing completed successfully, but I was a bit frustrated with that.

### Conclusion
I started writing this right after setting up and mounting the new one, so I haven't experienced any possible issues during workouts or while sleeping yet (especially with the significant change in size and weight), but so far, it's comfortable and doesn't feel much different.

The new action buttons on the left and the tap actions you can do with your index finger and thumb seem conveniently neat, and I'm inclined to use those more. However, for me, the main concern is how unobtrusive it is while continuously collecting health data. Battery longevity and new health data measurements are more important than new features. I knew about blood oxygen monitoring but wasn't aware of high blood pressure alerts, which I'm excited for. This makes it crucial to wear it constantly, minimizing the need to charge it frequently and reducing the need to remember to charge it before bed or heading out, which is my favorite part.

I'm looking forward to continuing to enjoy my Apple Watch.
