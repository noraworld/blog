### Introduction
As the title suggests, I participated in the House of Representatives election. My first time voting was in the Upper House Election in 2025, so this marks my second time. As for a House election, this is my first. Since it's a great opportunity, I thought I would document it for future reference. I'm writing this post right after I returned from voting while voting is ongoing, but it will be published after voting ends. My experience with the Upper House election is recorded here:

[I Voted in the 27th House of Councillors Election (2025)](https://noraworld.github.io/blog/upper-house-election-2025)

Last time, I didn't sleep from the day before the election until the day of, gathering last-minute information on YouTube before heading to vote. This time, I simply went to bed, and voted between 12:30 and 13:00. Last time was not because I wanted to pull an all-nighter to research; I just lost track of time and missed the chance to sleep. Now, I can't disrupt my weekday work schedule, unlike last time.

This time, I did gather some information lightly on YouTube, but didn't go as in-depth as before. Even if I research thoroughly, it's just one vote, and it's unlikely to influence much. Besides, politicians often don't keep their promises, so it's impossible to find a candidate that I'm 100% satisfied with. Regardless, I feel it’s important to vote to weaken block votes and express my own intentions, even if not everyone has to go (as some choose not to vote to express dissatisfaction with all parties).

Additionally, I’ve been thinking that elections are similar to watching sports. It’s like watching how the team (party) I support fares in the game (election). Particularly this election feels like a presidential election, where former PM Takaichi is querying the public if she is fit to remain as prime minister. Also, a new party, the Centrist Reform Union[^yuukoku] (misleadingly similar, though not involving the dissolution of the Constitutional Democratic Party or Komeito), has emerged, and I can’t predict what might happen, so I’m a bit excited about the results. In this kind of entertainment sense too, I feel like participating in this election is a good idea. There is no ultimate right answer and a vote is just a vote, so enjoying the entertainment aspect by treating it like a sporting event might be okay. Not that I’ve ever watched sports, though.

[^yuukoku]: Additionally, there is a new party, Tax Cuts for Japan and People’s Union, formed by a group who couldn’t endorse the Centrist Reform Union. It’s hard to see it as more than an election strategy, and I never intended to vote for them.

Honestly speaking, I chose my candidate through elimination. No, it might have been more a choice of compromise rather than elimination. The truth is, there was no candidate I felt compelled to vote for. The only candidate I’m familiar with is Masaaki Taira. I think he leans left and I have a negative impression of him. Since he is from the LDP, which I don’t want to support, I eliminated him.

Certainly, I can support Takaichi to some extent, but not this particular representative, and I had no intention of voting for the LDP anyway. The LDP spans left to right ideologies too broadly, and although a Takaichi continuation might mean a conservative trajectory, there’s ample chance a liberal faction could take over thereafter. Even Takaichi would likely need to appeal to liberals to a degree.

Even though I can somewhat support Takaichi and think a continuation is pragmatic and best, I can’t bring myself to vote for a candidate I don’t support personally just because they belong to the same party (the LDP). The LDP is likely to get numerous votes even without my contribution, so I don’t feel a strong desire to vote for them. It might not be for contrarian reasons, but voting for a minor party with views similar to mine feels more meaningful and interesting, so the LDP was excluded for various reasons.

Regarding the Communist Party, which I’ll discuss later, I opted out as it wasn’t an option for me, especially because I’m not familiar with the candidate.

This left the candidates from the Democratic Party for the People and Sannseitō. I think I’ve barely seen Masae Ido on a street poster before, but I don’t know her or her ideologies well. As for Etsuko Shima, I hadn’t heard of her name. At this point, you can’t vote based on candidate support but rather by choosing a party.

When deciding between the Democratic Party for the People and Sannseitō, I remembered the Democratic Party for the People being quite popular for a period but dwindled recently (popularity drop implied by behavior, not vote quantity). They are committed to increasing take-home pay, but other parties are saying similar things (even if they were the first to spread the idea and have been consistently doing so before election season). And since Tamaki, the party leader, is currently acting indecisively, my enthusiasm to support them wanes. Consequently, Sannseitō became my last option, leading me to vote for Shima Etsuko, despite being unfamiliar with her. It ended up being a very passive choice, but in single-member districts, choices are limited, so it couldn’t be helped.

On a party level rather than individual candidates, an additional reason is that Sannseitō is clearly conservative, matching political ideology with mine. In the spectrum of these four parties, Sannseitō was my final decision. It, too, was kind of passive, though.

On a total tangent, why do politicians often write their names in hiragana? Is it to seem approachable? Is it to set themselves apart from a certain country? Neither explanation fully clicks with me.

#### Proportional Representation
Here, you choose from all the parties, not candidates, so the reason behind my choice is slightly more positive. As mentioned, I voted for Team Mirai this time.

In the previous Upper House election, I voted for the Japan Conservative Party because politically, it aligned with my ideology. Additionally, I trusted Haruo Kitamura who could be one of the few relatively rational-minded among politicians, leading me to vote for him by name.

I haven’t withdrawn my support for the Conservative Party or Kitamura since then[^hoshuto]. Rather, I simply wanted to support Team Mirai this time.

[^hoshuto]: Though, to be fair, I wish their leader Hyakuta would watch his language more, and the candidate Koizumi whom I voted for last time seemed overly heated on an Abema TV program. I understood his feelings but wished he’d remained more composed as a politician.

The truth is, back during the Upper House election, I wasn’t aware of Team Mirai. To be precise, I might’ve heard of them, but I didn’t know anything about the party until later. Thus they weren’t an option for me then. However, I learned about Team Mirai later, watched their leader’s speeches on YouTube, and thought they made valid points (although politicians tend to act more put together in media, so I remain skeptical). Anno is an engineer-turned-politician, and I found his push for digital and AI development relatable too.

[^anno]: Anno was already known in the engineering world, so I knew of him — just not the party as of the last election.

Moreover, this time, while many parties emphasized sales tax and tax reduction, Team Mirai instead addressed insurance premium adjustments, which resonated with me (I think the Japan Restoration Party mentioned this too). Yes, insurance premiums are outrageously high. I recently learned that freelancers (with the National Health Insurance) pay premiums without company sharing the burden, unlike full-time employees (with Social Insurance), making them higher (there’s a “National Insurance Evasion” loophole some politicians exploit, apparently sparking controversy involving Japan Restoration Party members). Even without that, it’s simply high. Hardly visiting hospitals and yet premiums remain exorbitant, I find it hard to justify. For dental treatments, often insurance covers only inadequate services, pushing me to pay out-of-pocket, making insurance seem useless. Moreover, being universal health insurance, dropping out isn't an option (except escaping abroad), and it feels like just bearing old-age financial burdens, fueling constant frustration.

Additionally, this party avoids rigid boxes of left or right-wing camps, seeming judicious in decision-making. While imagining Takaichi continuing as PM is the most realistic, I have no intention of backing liberal vs. centrist power struggles. In that regard, favoring minor parties and distilling party power structures seems wise, leading me to Team Mirai, feeling it’s a sound investment.

I remain right-leaning politically still. But again, I’m not supportive of strengthening capitalism, which prioritizes earning more and enjoying greater luxury. In Japan, political conservatism and economic conservatism (capitalism) are often bundled, which complicates voting prioritization. While I support political conservatism, I resist economic conservatism and capitalism.

The opposite holds — economic parties opposing capitalism lean politically left. I oppose this too. There’s a political compass theory suggesting separating political and economic ideologies in modern politics beyond traditional left-right binaries.

There’s variance in classifying parties on this compass depending on perception (no definitive solution), but I personally support a party aligning with right-wing politics and left-wing economics (anti-capitalism, not synonymous with socialism/communism). Sadly, there’s no such party here in Japan. Japan’s left-leaning parties tend to be too pandering towards a certain nation.

Feeling frustrated with this lack, I lean towards parties outside that conventional framework (like how Team Mirai appears to be), matching on ideological points and thus choosing them. Honestly, had I known about them before the previous Upper House election, I might have chosen them instead of the Conservative Party. Curious to see their evolutionary trajectory, I hope to reflect on future elections from here.

### On the Communist Party (Communism)
Now, I’d like to mention the Communist Party (Communism) a bit. Back in the Upper House Election, I believed the Communist Party was the furthest from my options. Even now, my political ideologies remain incompatible, so nothing changed on that front. In this election, there’s a candidate from them in my single-member district, but there wasn't any significant decision required to exclude this option.

Yet, certain personal circumstances currently affect this perspective. That is, I just finished paying all my taxes and insurance for the 2025 fiscal year at the end of last January, and I struggled greatly financially until then. Almost all payments were overdue. At the end of January, my tax and insurance totaled over 400,000 yen.

I couldn’t pay on time as I hadn’t worked much last year, being without income over long periods. Realizing this unsustainable situation, I sought employment, but there was a long gap between resuming work and actual payment (monthly pay on next month-ends seemed incredibly distant) with daily expenses remaining. I couldn’t meet even credit card payments, experiencing temporary suspension and late penalties. Naturally, the same applied to taxes, concluding residence tax with a 3,000 yen delay charge.

In terms of credit cards, excessive delays automatically switch to revolving repayments, contracts could be canceled, or in worst scenarios, blacklists may develop, preventing new credit acquisitions or loans. Indeed, a couple of years ago (not last year), I reached the worst-case scenario with five cards suspended. I wrote about this here:

[5 Credit Cards Suspended - Considering Debit Card Solutions](https://noraworld.github.io/blog/debit-card-reunion)

Among those, one card still incurs hefty (>50% of total owed) interest monthly, yet unpaid, resembling revolving repayments. Once incoming remuneration’s cleared, I aim for immediate repayment; nonetheless, the looming pressure affected life and mental health. Such practices in financial industries using money to spawn further money unsettle me, directing an ongoing divorce from credit cards — but that story awaits another post.

Admittedly, my idleness to work accounted for these debts. However, my willingness to engage with the capitalist system is deficient. I feel strongly about being repulsed by this market-economy monetary valuation (basically numbers) for labor exchanges — topics for an in-depth post. In capitalism, everything’s quantifiable by profitability, dismissing non-profitable pursuits. Yet, non-profitable doesn’t translate to meaningless; contrarily, profitable cannot justify, like financial industries “generating” more without crafting substantial novelties.

Capitalism naturally channels money to moneyed hubs. While work environments might affect individual perceptions, structurally, an undeniable exploitation dynamic between capitalists and laborers exists to vary degrees, regardless of the term “exploitation’s” appropriateness.

And for those capitalist accumulations, laborers toil on. Now, a standard workweek in Japan spans 40 hours (sans overtime) for full-time employees. Allegedly, what’s truly necessary for survival constitutes much less, with surplus feeding capitalist profits.

Is money truly pivotal to this scale? Sure, some desire affluent lifestyles, valuing money, a sentiment I respect. However, capitalism enshrines and somewhat necessitates this notion socially, fostering my skepticism. Should you now work 40 hours weekly, but three suffice (ensuring viable living wages), how many would opt for a three-day week? Data's unfamiliar to me but I suspect some working three days a week long as affordably possible desire this more often. I certainly do.

Though freelancers perhaps differ, most companies wouldn’t support this on full-time contracts. Even among freelancers, firms contractually hiring four-to-five-day workers offer substantially fewer alternatives (in my knowledge).

Yet independent innovations and originality thrive from individual pursuits quoted in free time over disposable income; the modern era prizes free time for subjective prosperity, I believe. In essence, I value time far over the economical “number,” monetary digits than time access, viewing current weekly work hours as “overwork” (even without OT), subjectively.

Of course, these are personal sentiments — work provides joy for some, wanting more hours, which I respect too. Valuing diverse opinions implies reciprocal respect of those prioritizing time over money, yet policies and society fall short here, fueling dissatisfaction. The absence of viable alternatives (fewer) poses problems to me.

Such dissonance ties into motivational absence for work, causing recent financial distress and leading towards supporting the Communist Party as a gesture — not out of support, but desperation. This consideration was essentially reckless, spurred by despondency, reiterating my non-support for communism or political ideology aliments. Across economic and political spheres, other parties bolster capitalism, making communism almost sole solace economically.

Still, having cleared tax payments and somewhat settled circumstances, desperate considerations weakened, and opting out occurred regarding communist candidatures, both candidate and party-wise. Timing plays here since, if elections fell amidst financial chaos, impulsive vote decisions could arise, underscoring capitalism's frustrations.

I don’t repeatedly reemphasize communism/socialism support, but capitalism-valued constructs demand thought. As mentioned, no such valued-tent party exists in Japan. Communist-associated parties may politically align economically but political ideology remains discordant. Indeed, socialism as a whole doesn’t seem feasible either. I wish broader political distinction awareness regarding ideology like this increased.

### Old Media Decline and True Public Opinion
In the context of discussing elections, the relationship between left-wing governance and old media deserves mention. This may get lengthy, but here’s my view mingled with current politics concepts.

Apparently, the Centrist Reform Union, formerly known as the Constitutional Democratic Party, has a 0% support rate among youth. Whether it’s precisely 0% is unknown, but it’s undoubtedly low. Naturally, I don’t support them, and honestly, it’s perplexing (I’ll refrain from elaborating). This essentially means support primarily arises from middle-aged citizens and senior voters. I've long questioned why until reading Takeshi Iwasa’s recently published book 'True History of Korea: Why Can't They Abandon Anti-Japanese Sentiment?' It connected the dots[^book-progress].

[^book-progress]: I’m about 30% in, sufficient for contextual discussion here.

The old media’s tendency for biased reporting has grown noticeable, and this old media has been deceiving the public, backing liberal or rather pro-China/North Korea or anti-Japan political stances. Citizens who rely on solely consuming their content inevitably get tricked into voting for such parties.

Using "deceive" isn’t hyperbolic but reflects reality; they literally deceived the public. Older media and associated entities claimed absurdities like “North Korea kidnapping Japanese citizens is implausible, a right-wing conspiracy” or “North Korea is a workers’ paradise” or “(Missiles from North Korea) They’re peaceful satellites” or “The Korean War began as Korean aggression (a blatant falsehood)” — statements promulgated by both some media entities and political parties. Deluding citizens into voting for left-wing parties like the Socialist Party stemmed from this misinformation wave. Nowadays, spreading such obvious “lies” would backfire due to the Internet’s presence, foiling those schemes. Historically, an absence of the Internet made citizens gullible, relying solely on this outdated media, merely trusting it.

Although the book is rooted in history, focusing on Korea instead, it inadvertently illuminated present politics-media linkages.

Thus, some people still clinging to old media’s reports fueled said political support. Japan reportedly ranks markedly low for press freedom among developed countries, yet it also reportedly houses a populace with unusually high media credence, painting it as naive, metaphorically.

Additionally — this is conjecture-based analysis — one theory postulates old media adheres to this leftist bias likely due to harm inflicted by "occupation purge" during the GHQ control after WWII. This deserves detail, however, let’s keep it brief. The theory suggests post-WWII, America attempted placements that ensured Japan's incapacity to reescalate tensions, visible in their occupation policies.

Particularly, purging influential positions ostensibly sought removal of nationalistic figures exercising any major social control (educators, media personnel), ensuring left-leaning individuals filled those spaces. The internal purposes reflect America’s strategic calculus regarding post-war Japan.

Consequently, the embedded old-media propaganda aimed towards certain narratives seemingly facilitated by this “purge.” News venues and academia’s promotion of leftist biases during this era perhaps resulted from excluding notable pro-Japanese figures once deemed undesirable in such positions. That era spawned bizarre stances from having led over-propagated issues over time, disrupting logical consistency.

Thus contemporary ongoing extreme biases in leftist-purporting propaganda understandably depicts media history, firmly rooted within. Curiously, even some extreme-left outlets like Asahi Shimbun were advocates during pre-war towards extreme-right nationalist uprisings (The Mukden Incident). Neither stance sits favorably, to be fair.

While elements rooted in speculation arise, the tangential references via empirical representations here and examination of present situations show credible connectivity. Irrespective of total belief, barring hypothetical leftist political alliance empowerment or unexpected elections, destroying organized vote concentration remains a tangible effort — fostering minimal beneficial outcomes embracing variety across limited youth respects outlining alternative paths youth impact perspectives.

### Voting Environment and Afterwards
The voting venue matched last election’s but seemed to have an alternate entrance. It was held at an elementary school, a large venue with multiple entries, apparently. Unlike last time, I approached to find this entrance shuttered, leaving me slightly confused. An on-site lady kindly directed me — “For the election? It’s around this corner” — sending others similarly misdirected my way too. Others in the same boat voiced understanding, heading towards the appropriate entrance, which felt a bit perplexing for many. Though there were printed signs, they failed to capture clear direction, inducing unintended yet unnecessary diversions.

Meanwhile, Tokyo experienced snowfall today. Up till today, snowfall predominantly occurred on January 2nd and yesterday, February 7th, resembling sleet[^sleet-ref]. Yet today showed accumulation. I awoke at half-past eleven only to draw open curtains finding snow both descending and settled, a remarkable surprise. Despite negligible snowfall until now, today seemed exceptionally snowy for Election Day. Unsurprisingly chilly — maintaining a voting entrance ticket over my belongings (no bag), wielding an umbrella devoid of gloves – rendered hands particularly frozen.
