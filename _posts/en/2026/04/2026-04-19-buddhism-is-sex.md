It's because anyone who says "Buddha will get angry at you for saying that!" or "That's disrespectful to a sacred religion!" doesn't understand Buddhism at all. I can say it confidently because I do understand it completely: **Buddhism is like sex.**

## Do You "Know" Sex?
Do you all "know" what the act of sex is?

You might think, "Everyone knows that, we're not kids."

But there might be people who think, "I don't know sex because I'm a virgin."

If you think that, and you haven't reached enlightenment yet, opening enlightenment might not be that hard for you. Because that is the essence of Buddhism (Eastern philosophy).

This means that logically, interpreted in words, it goes as follows:

As an adult, everyone knows what the act of "sex" is in words (conceptual knowledge). However, some adults have never actually had sex. Meaning there are adults who haven't experienced it firsthand (experiential knowledge).

If we define "knowing" as the former, practically no adults on this planet don't "know" sex. However, if we define "knowing" as only after experiencing it, then there are adults who don't "know" sex (like me).

The latter, the "knowing" that comes from experience, which is called "enlightenment" in Japanese, does not matter. Words are just words, and no matter how much you understand through words, unless you experience it, there is an "essence" that cannot be grasped.

Anyone who has actually had sex understands this well. No matter how much knowledge you have about what sex is or how to enhance pleasure, unless you experience it, you can never truly understand that feeling of pleasure that comes with sex.

## An Organization for Experiential Understanding
This also applies to Buddhism (Eastern philosophy). You can gather as much information about Eastern philosophy or Buddhism from articles or books online. However, unless you have a strong "experience" in your mind that shouts, "Ah!!! That's what life's (sufferings) are about!!!!" you can never achieve enlightenment, no matter how much you understand or build knowledge from words (conceptual knowledge).

And annoyingly, that's why it's impossible to express this in words and make everyone understand. Buddhism was created with the purpose of allowing people to gain experiential understanding, and sometimes it would intentionally say unscientific things, lies, or contradictions as a way of teaching (lies for convenience).

However, dismissing Buddhism as "just a religion" or "unscientific and occult-like" means you'll never truly understand Buddhism. Conversely, even if you become a devout believer, that doesn't guarantee you'll understand the "truth."

Moreover, you can understand the truth without joining, obeying precepts, or worshiping Buddha. (In fact, Buddha reportedly denied such mystical things.)

## How to Achieve Enlightenment? (How to Have Sex?)
To experience the pleasure of sex (enlightenment), you need to actually have sex. Therefore, Buddhism had the aim of teaching "how to have sex" to Buddha's disciples at the time, and that's what's written in the scriptures and books.

And, of course, this "how to have sex" differs from person to person. First of all, you can't start without a partner, biologically speaking, or in terms of language definition (assuming we're not calling solitary acts or imagination "sex"). In that sense, there's no dispute that finding a partner is the first step.

So, how do you find a partner? If you tell someone with no opposite gender at their workplace, "office romance is good," it means nothing. If someone doesn't own a smartphone, or doesn't know how to use one (who knows how many people like this exist today), saying "try a dating app" is meaningless.

That's why Buddha taught methods for "opening enlightenment" that match each individual. In the previous example, Buddha might have recommended office romance to some or dating apps to others.

If you interpret only the "you should do this" part as a "fact" applicable to everyone, naturally, "contradictions" will exist. But that's not the essence. As long as "the person" in front of them achieves their goal, Buddha was okay with that.

## Why Do We Become Unhappy?
For most humans, sex is literally "something they want to do." Because of that, by finding special value in the act of "sex," people feel upset about their appearance or environment when they cannot achieve it, feeling unhappy. But that's an illusion.

For instance, in the past, people didn't eat tomatoes. They thought tomatoes were the Devil's fruit, i.e., poisonous. Now it's common knowledge that tomatoes are safe to eat, and they're widely available, so they don't seem special anymore.

Now, imagine you time-traveled to a period when tomatoes were deemed the Devil's fruit and were not supposed to be eaten in your region (even though you knew they were safe you'd have to follow this rule). Despite tomatoes not being special, you would think about them more and more...

The Caligula effect, which means the more you're told "don't," the more you want to do it, is a well-known psychological phenomenon.

For almost everyone, tomatoes weren't special. Yet, once time-traveled, you'd see tomatoes everywhere and realize they are safe to eat! "Oh, why can't I eat tomatoes, even though I know they're okay... I'm so unfortunate!"

And just before that frustration builds up, you travel back to the present. You see tomatoes sold normally, and you think, "Thank goodness... I can finally eat tomatoes!"

Human unhappiness works this way. By attributing special value to certain things, you feel unfortunate about not obtaining or accomplishing them.

### Money is an Illusion
The tomato story uses time travel for illustration, and honestly, in Japan, if you really want to experience sex, you could visit a brothel. Plus, many have already experienced it without visiting one.

However, what about money? When you place high value on money, which is essentially just numbers in your bank account, and don't obtain it, that non-fulfillment causes unhappiness.

But in reality, money is paper. Or thin metal. Or in the current cashless era, not even a tangible object. Just numbers. Those are given value only by humans (at least of all creatures known as of now). Even then, money really has gone to being "just paper" several times in history due to hyperinflation.

If that's the case, then the fact that changes in money, this abstract thing, toss us from joy to despair is proof our brains are showing us an illusion.

People could easily grasp this to some degree. Anyone who can read Japanese should understand this passage. Thus, it's possible for anyone to conceptually grasp this idea.

It's natural for modern people to think "Even so, without money, we can't live!" And that's the most challenging part of Eastern philosophy—it makes it difficult to reach "enlightenment" merely through words or text.

Think about it. The value a sex worker places on sex can't be the same as that of a virgin. Yet, it's true that some virgins seriously struggle with this aspect.

But when you truly grasp this (when you become enlightened in the true sense), all unhappiness disappears at once.
