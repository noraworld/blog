## Philosophy of Emptiness and the Heart Sutra
Buddha refers to the enlightened figure who achieved enlightenment. When Buddha reached this state, he realized that everything in the universe is formed by interconnectedness. Later, Nagarjuna, who correctly understood Buddha's enlightenment, developed this truth into the "Philosophy of Emptiness."

The core of this philosophy is summarized in the Heart Sutra. It essentially means that things you think "exist" might actually be "non-existent."

For example, what we call a "bicycle" doesn't really have its own essence. It's just parts like handlebars, a seat, and wheels put together in a way that forms a vehicle for traveling. If you take it apart, it stops being a bicycle.

Even further, those handlebars, seat, and wheels are just materials like metal or rubber shaped in a certain way. There's no real essence to a handlebar, seat, or wheel. Smash the handlebar, and it loses its identity as a handlebar.

This applies to not only objects but also causality and physical phenomena. We might think "Ball A hit Ball B," but it's also possible to say, "B was there when A was flying."

Gravity too. We see an apple falling to Earth as if the Earth is pulling it down, but really, the apple is also pulling the Earth (as per the law of universal gravitation). The law depends on mass, so Earth seems to pull more because it’s massively larger than an apple, making it convenient for us to think of it as gravity. In daily life, it's just easier, and that's why we call it gravity.

Thus, "bicycles," "cause and effect," and "gravity" don't really exist. They're words we use to describe parts of the universe. A collection of handlebars, seats, and wheels making something we ride we call a bicycle. When moving things affect stationary things, we think there's a cause, and when we see apples falling to Earth, we label it as gravity for convenience.

This applies to ourselves too. We often think, "I'm suffering because of my situation," or "I'm unlucky because of what's happened," but that's just our brain's perspective. Suffering and misfortune aren't real. Ultimately, if you think you’re unhappy, then you’re unhappy because you’ve defined it so in your mind.



## Not Knowing "Unhappiness" Can Make You The Strongest
Infants likely don't understand the concept of "the apple on a plate on a table." It's not just because they don't know the words "table," "plate," and "apple," but they can’t perceive the boundaries between them. Understanding words creates boundaries and helps us differentiate.

A newborn baby perhaps doesn't feel unhappy in any seemingly unpleasant situation or with any harsh parents. The baby doesn't understand the idea of being unhappy because it has no concept of it. They have no basis for comparison, as they don't know anything else. Once you understand unhappiness, you match it with your situation and decide if you’re unhappy or not. But that's just a trick our brain plays; it isn't real.

So, is there no hope for us adults who already know all this? If we think we're unhappy, are we stuck with it forever...?

No. Even adults can realize this truth, stop dividing by using words, remove boundaries, and see the universe as one single entity, thereby ceasing to be unhappy. They can no longer define unhappiness. Without a definition, you can’t be unhappy.

This means reaching a state like a newborn’s world without words, ultimate ignorance, or eternal beginner's mind, allowing us to experience the same liberation from suffering that Buddha (the enlightened one) did.
