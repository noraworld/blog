## The Modern Version of 'Impermanence'
I tweeted something like this a while back.

> Ultimately, if you consider that all the intellectual activities (white-collar work) you're struggling with now could be completely replaced if AI explodes in development, then there's no point in "struggling to achieve something." Just doing what you want at the moment, in other words, "living by doing what you love" is probably the happiest way to live as a human.
>
> I said "intellectual activities," but that's only because it's easier to imagine with the current state of AI. As time progresses, autonomous robots (androids) with AI will likely emerge, and physical activities (blue-collar work) will be replaceable too. If every activity becomes replaceable or much easier, what's the point of struggling (doing what you don't want to do) to achieve something?
>
> The modern "impermanence" might be this: Not just "things with form will eventually break (→ so cherish this moment)," but "all human activities will eventually be replaceable (→ so cherish what you want to do now)." Therefore, the only way to decide whether to do something is if it's what you want to do. If you can't attach any added value like "I made this, I did this," then there's no other way to live without struggling.

_[Insight](https://misskey.io/notes/al545vjlocdv09h3)_

I'm not denying "struggling." So, perhaps I should have expressed "struggle" as "doing something even when it causes unnecessary pain or discomfort."

Anyway, I've come to understand that this is what life is ultimately about.



## Nothing is Permanent
About 2,500 years ago, Buddha realized: "You can't be certain of getting what you want (even with effort), and what's here now will eventually be lost (you can't ensure it will last forever). Clinging to things creates suffering (the attachment to getting what you want because you believe you won't be happy without it, and the attachment to never wanting to let go of precious things you already have, like money, status, fame, loved ones). So, live for the now."

I think this message is even more important now. As AI has advanced so much, especially people in white-collar jobs might worry, "Will I lose my job if AI keeps developing?"

On the other hand, people in blue-collar jobs might feel confident, thinking, "This is something only humans can do, no matter how much AI advances."

From the viewpoint of impermanence, both thoughts are incorrect. In the Edo period, there were professional couriers who delivered letters and packages; now, that job doesn't exist, replaced by truck drivers and other roles.

At the turn of the ages, those who clung to their courier jobs may have lost their way, but those who accepted change and learned driving skills didn't miss out on work.

Times are always changing. Nothing is permanent. Even Buddha from 2,500 years ago understood that. With the rapid progress of science and technology today, including AI, it’s even truer.

Fear of change is human nature, but clinging to it creates suffering. While it's understandable to be scared, you can't stop the change. Some people try to halt it by appealing to politics, but even that won't stop it. It's harsh, but that's reality.

Therefore, the key is accepting this unchangeable reality, embracing new values with courage, and thinking, "Even if I lose this current life, it's okay."

Famous historical figures understood this well.

> The only thing we should practice is letting go. Anyone can cling to things. You don't need to learn that.

_Rilke_

> Some think clinging makes us stronger, but sometimes letting go does.

_Hermann Hesse_

> We must be willing to let go of the life we planned so as to have the life that is waiting for us.

_Joseph Campbell_



## You Can't Prove Today's Struggles
That's why doing what you truly want now (what you find enjoyable) is the only way to live happily. Even if you accomplish something with struggle and pain when you don't really want to, what does it mean? The day may come when future AI (equipped in autonomous robots or androids) accomplishes it in an instant.

Ultimately, in the future, you won't be able to completely prove, "This was something I struggled to make back then!" Future AI might perfectly mimic it, and technologies to prove the time of creation might be broken (if quantum computers come, all current encryption will likely be broken).

It's impossible to definitely prove your current struggle in the future... If that's the case, there's no point in clinging to struggle. Regret about "What was all that struggle for...?" will literally come.

That's why now is important. Live in the moment, and by connecting these continuous moments, a truly happy future awaits. It's not about saying, "Everything is pointless, so give up and live lazily." Surely everyone has something they truly want to do (something other than just idling about). But maybe it's just not visible because you're busy with the "things you don't want to do" in front of you.

Even if you think, "There are things I have to do (but don't want to) in front of me," you're probably making one of these assumptions: "It's actually possible to find enjoyment in it, but you mistakenly think you don't want to because of attachment," or "It's not actually something you have to do for your life (you mistakenly think you have to)."

I won't go into specifics of each situation. Everyone's circumstances (lives) are too different to know what's right, and even if someone tells you what to do, you won't be satisfied with any outcome. You have to decide for yourself.

But if you live according to this mindset, you should be satisfied with whatever results may come. It could be deciding to quit your job or a decision to end a relationship. No matter the outcome, as long as it's a decision you confidently believed in, you won't have regrets.

Conversely, if the decision is full of attachment to appearances or pride, no matter how much it garners societal praise or the envy of those around you, you'd only be left with a sense of emptiness, thinking, “Something is amiss.”

Realizing your true self is not about living for others' evaluations or clinging to current stability, but about living by doing what you genuinely want.



## Inequality is Here to Stay
Even while working hard and struggling, the rich might be sipping wine elegantly in the penthouse of a luxury condo, and politicians might be engaged in corruption.

But if you're a Japanese reader, you already have, from birth, the right to enjoy good safety, job stability, and fulfilling welfare and entertainment for life (it's highly probable). Foreigners might envy you for having that without any struggle.

Medieval aristocrats indulged in such lavishness that even corrupt politicians today can’t compare.

However, modern people can travel the world even without being incredibly wealthy. Even if aristocrats of that time wanted the same, undeveloped navigation would leave them uncertain of reaching their destination—always risking death by shipwreck amidst the rough seas. If historical aristocrats who desired to travel the world saw today, they might envy ordinary people.

A generation ago, there were no washing machines, refrigerators, or vacuums, so housework had to be done by hand. Elders who survived those times might say, "This is why today's youth are so lazy and degenerate. In my youth, we struggled, and so on."

And this will continue. By the time today's young people become elders, AI may advance so much that people won't need to work directly. Then, today's young people (future elders) might think, "This is why today's youth are so lazy and degenerate. In my youth, we struggled, and so on."

Inequality is forever. You can't change the circumstances you're born into. Let alone change the era you're born in, so it's impossible to eradicate inequality, even if a perfectly equal society is achieved. If lamenting inequality changes nothing, then the only option is accepting it and finding your happiness.

No matter how hard you work, you might never beat someone born into extreme wealth (born to a very rich family) in terms of money, and no matter how much you improve yourself, you might not be more attractive than naturally handsome or beautiful people.

But is that what you truly want? Is defeating someone born extremely wealthy your dream? Is your goal to attract anyone and everyone instead of finding someone you love?

If you truly believe so, that's fine. Fulfill that. But if not, comparing yourself to others and feeling inequality is akin to seeking unhappiness. That won’t make money fall from the sky, nor will it suddenly make you handsome tomorrow.



## Happiness is Permanent
In the end, no matter how you think about it, everyone will never get the same things, and no matter how much time progresses (whether 2,500 years ago, now, or in the future), you can't enjoy future wealth now, and inequality will never disappear, no matter how clean politics gets.

If that’s the case, rather than desperately striving for the same environment as everyone else, finding your happiness is the only non-regretful (non-suffering) path.

And fortunately, as long as you change your mindset, happiness can surely be achieved in any circumstance. You don't need to be rich to be happy, and looks don’t determine happiness. What's important is accepting reality, listening to your true feelings, and bravely moving forward. As long as you hold onto this mindset and don’t misinterpret it, your inner wisdom (true desire) will never betray you.

When you truly understand this (realize it), you will no longer be swayed by anything. Not even by illusions created by your mind (fears, anxieties, sadness, jealousy, anger, shame, disappointment, etc.).
