## Science Can Only Take the Long Way Around and Never Arrive at Absolute Answers
The benefits of Eastern philosophy are also being scientifically proven, and we hear things like "mindfulness is good" or "meditation improves mental well-being." Having reached enlightenment, I completely understand now, and it feels like, "well, that's obvious" (without even needing scientific backing).

It's like, in mathematics, Eastern philosophy uses a method like 5 (meditation) + 5 (interdependence) to arrive at 10 (mental improvement), while science erratically wonders what can be added (what method to use) to reach the result of 10 (mental improvement).

If you were to prove this within the scope of natural numbers "scientifically," there would only be 5 ways: 1 + 9, 2 + 8, 3 + 7, 4 + 6, 5 + 5 (ignoring the order), but without the premise of natural numbers, you could say, "well, what if it's 0.5 + 9.5?" or claim the possibility of "0.01 + 9.99," and it becomes tangled.

This is akin to saying "meditation is good for mental health," but adding, "blindly believing meditation will absolutely improve mental health is not good" or "using meditation to escape from immediate unpleasantness becomes evasion which backfires."

Having reached enlightenment, I find myself thinking "well, that's just obvious," but this "obvious" doesn't pass in science. Because it requires explanations based on discriminating knowledge (understanding things through words or logic) and must be "proven" before accepted.

## Fundamental Differences Between Western Philosophy (Science) and Eastern Philosophy
Science, having evolved from Western philosophy, is a field of study trying to know the phenomena in the outer world of human beings (accurately "my conscious experience"). Hence, science values objective facts. In Western philosophy and science, if you can explain a theory in your own words, it’s considered understood.

In contrast, Eastern philosophy is about understanding the inside of a human being ("what is self?"), and this understanding only occurs through a powerful "experience" in the mind, like "Aha! I get it!" rather than theories or words. In Japanese, to differentiate from the former (understanding with words or theories), this is called "satori" or enlightenment.

Therefore, in Eastern philosophy, no matter how well it is explained with words, unless you've reached enlightenment, it's not considered understood. And nobody can objectively judge whether someone else has reached enlightenment. Even if someone says "I've attained enlightenment," they might not have, and those who have might not appear different externally (to those around them).

And, since it's a philosophy about understanding the inside of human beings, by reaching enlightenment, you can understand questions like "why do I feel suffering?" or "why does unhappy stuff keep happening (which I believe)?" Conversely, no matter how much someone tells you the reasons, unless you experience that "Aha!" moment in your mind (attain enlightenment), you won't truly understand it.

## The Limit of Science
This is the limit of science (Western philosophy). Science, only observing the outside world of “my conscious experience,” will never know "the reason 'I' suffer" from objective observation. It's a problem inherent in the nature of science.

This applies to neuroscience as well. One might think, "Mindfulness, meditation, and zazen are scientifically proven to be effective," but it's merely a result of observing the human brain as an organ "objectively from the outside" and will, by no means, get to know why "my conscious experience" feels suffering or how to eliminate it. This, due to the nature of science, will not change even with the advancement of science and technology in the future.

However, if you reach enlightenment through an intense experience, it's a different story. In the case of "my conscious experience," Eastern philosophy knows more than science (even in the future when science and technology develop further). It's possible to create new methodologies to become mentally invincible. The meditation and zazen that ancestors have created in the past, precisely reflect this.

Science will not go beyond determining whether existing ideas are effective or not, "how effective they are," and usually only "how to make them even more effective?" seeing only the outside (the brain as an organ objectively).

Frankly, it's inefficient and can’t provide any answers to universal human questions like "how can we live happily?". Even with future advances in neuroscience, its highest achievement might be "being able to cure Alzheimer's" (which would indeed be great and we would want that to happen).

## "If You Do This, This Happens" Causality Doesn't Exist in This World
In the world of science, proving causation is often exceedingly difficult. Is exercise good for health itself, or is it because people who exercise tend to take care of their health? It’s hard to say. The classic "Which came first, the chicken or the egg?" It's only a probability that exercise likely has health benefits; it's unproven 100%.

Yet to anyone who learns Eastern philosophy, such things are "just obvious." Why? Because, **from the start, causality simply doesn't exist in this world (it's just a human misconception).**

Ball A flying and hitting ball B—is the cause A flying, or B being there to get hit? Nobody knows, and there cannot be such causality to begin with. "Hey, you just hit me!" and "It's your fault for being where I was going!"—neither is right, neither can be proven wrong. "Causality," "correct," and "incorrect" don’t exist.

Having determined "existence" without any proof, naturally, this occurs. Causality simply doesn't "exist" in this world. Arguing about whether it's the hitter or the blocker's fault is pointless.

So the scientific "Do this, and this happens" (like "Exercise makes you healthier" or "Eating red meat raises cancer risk")—frankly fearing no misunderstanding—is merely a shared belief among many in modern society "scientifically known things." At best, it's probability. Bringing in someone who never exercises, getting them to start might make them healthier often enough to say, "Exercise seems likely beneficial." That's science's limitation.

Frankly, even without any scientific evidence, a far-fetched theory believed earnestly can indeed make someone healthier or improve their mental state (placebo effect). Conviction such as "Exercise is good for your health!" might lead to actual health benefits, while doing it thinking, "This is such a hassle, might as well not bother..." could actually make someone less healthy.

Since "Exercise is good for you" has become almost scientifically certain (probabilistically) and common sense, many get healthier through exercise. It means many just benefit from this placebo effect, and if it weren't widespread, results might be different.

Moreover, if many read this and think, "Oh, turns out exercise might not be good after all," the more this spreads, the less healthy exercise becomes (if this truly happens, I apologize 🙇‍♂️ But this also means, if you believe what science deems unhealthy isn’t, it might actually make you healthier—it’s not entirely impossible).
