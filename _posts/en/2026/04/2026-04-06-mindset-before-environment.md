Recently, I've been thinking about Buddhist ideas, and I've realized that being mindful of the way we think is really important.

Think about working for a good company remotely versus a company that requires you to go to the office. Many people prefer remote work because commuting is a waste of time, riding crowded trains is stressful, working in a tense office atmosphere is not comfortable, and not being able to do chores like laundry and dishes during work breaks feels inefficient. For such people, having to go to the office is nothing but a pain.

However, some people like face-to-face communication. Having discussions in person can strengthen relationships, and you may hear unexpected good news during casual chats. There are definitely benefits.

The crucial thing is not just that remote work is more efficient or productive for your lifestyle (though that might be important too), but how you emotionally approach your external environment. If you are dissatisfied with your current situation (like not wanting to go to the office but having to because of your job), many people (myself included) tend to think that changing the environment will solve the issues. In other words, if we don't change the environment, we'll continue to suffer from dissatisfaction and stress.

But, if we flip this thinking and focus on how we feel, other options can appear. We can think about how to feel comfortable in the situation of having to go to the office, and figure out what we can do to make unpleasant emotions less frequent.

Of course, changing the environment is one solution. If you're miserable having to go to the office, finding a company that allows remote work could solve that. If this addresses most of your job dissatisfaction, then it's a good option.

However, sometimes changing your environment is not easy. Finding a new job might be achievable, but wanting to never work again and suddenly have $10 million in cash is unrealistic for most people. Constantly feeling discontent because you can't achieve that only leads to more suffering.

Furthermore, in the example of going to the office or not, if the only complaint is having to commute, switching to a remote-friendly company might fix it. But humans are complicated, and it's rarely that simple. Most people understand that even if you change jobs or environments, without changing your mindset, you may soon feel dissatisfied about something else. Low salary, annoying colleagues, long overtime hours, the pressure to respond quickly on Slack, not getting along with your boss... The cycle of changing jobs or environments often stems from this mindset.

Of course, in cases like working for a toxic company or being abused by a family member, changing your environment is critical. But if it's not that serious, it's important to change your mindset first. Instead of directly altering the environment causing dissatisfaction, try finding ways to see things positively.

There are people who prefer working at the office. Complaining about having to commute only makes you suffer more and doesn't solve anything. Even if you can change your environment, unless you change the way you think, you'll find something else to be dissatisfied with and remain stressed.

If you dislike commuting, try seeing the train ride as a good opportunity to read books you wouldn't at home. Increase casual chats with coworkers on purpose or get off a stop early to walk part of the commute home. Complaining alone won't change anything. Realizing this, think about what little things you could do to make life a bit more positive, and you may find a new path.

The means will be different for each person, and if you try one thing and don't feel satisfied, think of something else to try. Through these efforts, you might establish a style that feels comfortable to you. Over time, the stress of having to go to the office, once a strong dissatisfaction, might suddenly be gone, and you'll be able to live a comfortable life.

Although I'm writing confidently, I'm still on this journey myself, actively trying to adopt and focus on this mindset. In the world, it's easy to feel that things are unfair, and there are times when you may feel frustrated wondering why you have to face hardships while others don't.

However, there are people who grew up in more difficult environments, or are in less favorable social positions, yet seem happier than I am. When I think about the difference between them and myself, I realize it often comes down to the mindset. It's easy to complain about reality, but staying stuck in that thinking only hurts yourself. I'm finally starting to understand that.
