## Western Philosophy is Interesting, Too
Along with Eastern philosophy, I've become interested in Western philosophy. I just learned the basics of Hume and Kant's philosophy. This made me realize that, even among humans, we see different worlds, so it's impossible to share exactly the same thing.

## We Don't See the Same World
There are people who are born blind. It’s not that hard to understand that those people see, perceive, and feel the world differently than those who aren’t visually impaired.

However, we often assume that if we're not visually impaired, we see the same world. There are people whose "color-sensing cells" in their eyes are inherently weak, and they can’t distinguish the color between "fresh red meat" and "red meat that's starting to spoil."

Even if we are supposed to be "seeing" the same thing, "this is starting to spoil" might not be a shared understanding. If you can't judge with your own eyes, you only have two options: trust what those who can judge (or seem to be able to judge) say, or question if it's really true.

Moreover, you can't know "what someone thinks or feels" seeing something. Someone might not think much about seeing a person roll up their sleeves, while another might see bare arms and think, "That's sexy..." (This is what we call a fetish). For instance, two guys might both find a girl attractive but feel differently about the way she rolls up her sleeves.

## We Don't Hear the Same World
The same applies to hearing as well as seeing. Of course, there are differences in hearing ability among people, and as we age, the range of frequencies (hertz) we can hear typically narrows. So, it's easy to understand that some people can or can't hear the mosquito tone outside a store.

However, even if they can hear it, how they perceive it might not be the same. A famous example is that the sound of crickets is an autumnal tradition for Japanese people, giving them a sense of emotion, while foreigners might only hear them as the noise of insects.

Of course, some Japanese people might perceive it as noise, while some foreigners might find it atmospheric. And it might not only be Japanese people. Nevertheless, even if people hear the same sound, some might recognize it as "the sound of insects," while others see it simply as "insect noise."

If that's the case, when people hear the same sound, some might think, "It's a nice sound," while others might think, "It's noisy." Naturally, it's easy to imagine that those thinking, "It's a nice sound," live with less stress.

Even if it might be tough for someone who thinks "it's noisy" to suddenly start thinking "it's a nice sound," at the very least, having this mindset might lighten the mental load.

## What Happiness Is Isn't the Same for Everyone
Thinking like this makes you realize that what you’ve always thought was "obvious," or "universal truths," are actually just assumptions and outcomes based on your life experiences. Even things that seem certain, either visible or audible, can be perceived differently by different people. So, what you think, feel, or even, "What is happiness?" isn’t absolute.

## Science and Math Can’t Prove the Universe’s Truths
Still, while there might be people who see red meat as blue or the sound of crickets as the noise of an ambulance, such people, generally, don’t exist (at least as a general rule). So, it’s acceptable that, regardless of your life path or where you live, humanity has a loose shared understanding of things.

This foundation led to the establishment of sciences and mathematics. However, these are still based on the assumption of a "loose shared understanding.” They don’t necessarily reveal the universe’s absolute truths. For instance, science assumes causality. But if there’s no causality, then even if it’s scientifically correct, it might not be the universe's truth. And it's impossible to prove. This is why science can’t reach the universal truth (or at least prove that it has reached it).

Not so long ago, I wrote “[The More You Trust Science, The Further You Get From Happiness](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n627634cdb19a)”, but at that point, I didn’t know Western philosophies by Hume or Kant (I only knew their existence and names). Before learning about Hume’s philosophy, I had drawn the same conclusion Hume did. But since I knew Eastern philosophical concepts before writing that article, it wasn’t surprising to reach the same conclusion. With fundamental thinking, you can reach the same conclusions as esteemed philosophers, which is fascinating.

## Life Lessons Seen Through Philosophy
Going further, asking, "What should we learn from this?" seems like the difference between Western and Eastern philosophy. Western philosophy might declare, "That's why it's crucial to further work and think tirelessly to find humanity’s absolute truths.” Meanwhile, Eastern philosophy suggests, "Because everyone’s version of what’s right is different (there’s no absolute correctness), people suffer because they think 'this is the truth, therefore humans should do this, the world should be like this.' To be free from suffering, it's important to admit that everything is just an assumption." They reached the same theoretical conclusions, but perhaps the life lessons learned were different.

Philosophy is interesting. For me, who had believed absolutely in science, finding philosophy was quite shocking. I experienced the destruction of the foundational values I had held as absolute. This is just an assumption, but in the ancient times when supernatural phenomena (like earthquakes or thunder) were genuinely believed to be caused by gods, the shock of being told "There are no gods" might have felt like this (even though you can’t prove "there are no gods," as well).

Simultaneously, realizing how tough it is to doubt things widely believed today makes you ponder. If I said, "Maybe the earth isn't round!" now, I’d surely be seen as crazy. Still, it’s a fact that you can’t absolutely say, "The earth is definitely round. This is the universe’s absolute truth and never altering.” After all, that's within the understanding of humans who can only recognize three-dimensional space. If we had senses that could recognize beyond human capability, maybe there’d be something fuzzy around the earth and it wouldn’t be a sphere...

We can’t truly know what’s real (or maybe such a truth doesn’t even exist), so unless philosophizing itself is fun, we might as well just do whatever feels good to us now. Life might just be like that.

By writing it down, it sounds rather cheap. Maybe it's just that my writing skills aren't good... But feeling this makes life definitely easier.
