As it went off track, I decided to make it a separate article. I recommend reading that one first.

## Work and Money Are Just Tools for Survival
I'm currently working as a freelance software engineer, but it's not what I really want to do. I enjoy writing blog posts like this more, even if it doesn't make me money. In the future, I'd love to make a living by creating content, even if it's not writing.

To do that, I need time. Honestly, I don't care much about money. To me, money is just a piece of paper, an ambiguous number that's only a medium to keep living. So, working five days a week feels unbearable.

## A Momentary High
I worked with an agency to find freelance jobs that require only about three days of work per week. Apparently, there were other candidates interested in the position, and the hiring manager was torn, but in the end, they chose me.

So, my five-day-a-week contract, which was due to end in late March, wrapped up, and from April, I was supposed to live my dream three-day workweek and enjoy plenty of free time...

But what happened next was surprising. Just like when I had changed jobs in the past, my motivation disappeared. Now, even with a three-day workweek, I'm still demotivated, which is weird given how much I wanted this.

### Reason ①: Pressure from Attachment
I know why. One reason was the immense pressure.

I mentioned that my agent found me a three-day workweek job, but in reality, these are quite rare for software engineers. Most opportunities require five full days. There are also few like this one, where the positions are limited or require high-level skills and might be temporary with a set deadline, like "Revise this feature, and you're done."

So, having secured this rare opportunity, I couldn't afford to lose it, prompting me to think, "I must work sincerely." Here, the emphasis is on "sincerely" and "attachment."

Frankly, I don't want to work. Whether employed or freelance, I find no fundamental motivation for being hired for work. Ultimately, it's just for survival. As I previously mentioned, I want to live by creating things from imagination. This job is merely a stopgap until I achieve that. If I could make a living with my creative endeavors, I'd quit without hesitation.

Therefore, I find it hard to work "sincerely." I end up working lackadaisically, mixing work with personal stuff.

Working five-day weeks left me too tired to engage in creative work after hours, and even the weekends weren't enough. Weekday work required me to wake up early, leaving me exhausted and wanting to sleep more. This was stressful, and it's why I worked on personal things during work hours.

However, with a three-day workweek, I thought I could change, working sincerely for those days and keeping personal life separate. Since I felt I'd regret losing the job if I wasn't diligent, pressure built up so much that it became mentally exhausting. Before, during work hours, I would simply clock in unless leaving for something significant; now, I was meticulous about reporting exact work hours, which I never did before since I worked casually. Knowing this added to my stress.

### Reason ②: Too Much Freedom
That's the first reason, and the second was "too much freedom."

Until March, I had a set schedule, with a meeting every morning at 10, keeping my work hours from 10 to 19. Despite being a night owl, this motivated me to sleep early, despite wanting to stay up late.

The new job had no set hours. As long as I worked the equivalent of three eight-hour days a week, it was fine. This resulted in poor self-discipline with routines. Despite the condition being more freeing, which should have been welcome, it threw off my rhythm, leading to a stark nocturnal pattern, being active from 9 AM to 5 PM.

Although I've lived nights before, this sudden change made me realize how much routine work and school provide for keeping consistent sleep cycles. Although or perhaps because of my strong nocturnal tendencies, losing that structure without work hours reverting to natural cycles could be seen positively.

Whatever the case, working from night into late hours wasn't appealing, leading to day-trading personal time. Coupled with needing to "work sincerely," accumulating 24 hours weekly proved stressful as I found myself unable to reach that threshold, resorting to working additional days to cover slack.

### Reason ③: Sparse Human Connections
Lastly, the major factor contributing to the loneliness mentioned in ["A Pathetic Man's Rant"](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n99175636a917) is "sparse human connections."

There were no mandatory meetings. There was an optional 30-minute meeting once a week with full-time staff, but freelancers like myself weren't required to attend. Initially, I attended to know people but realized it wasn't enough.

I've experienced drastic declines in work motivation stemming from sparse personal connections. Although tedious, meetings kept me engaged. Work felt directionless, akin to tending weeds in a desert nobody visits. Except for survival, clawing through freelance work made me wonder, what else was there?

## Loneliness
As someone with few acquaintances, sparseness at work proved fatal. Beyond work motivation, avoiding human contact from virtually no interactions grew increasingly isolating. Social media also felt unappealing and useless.

Perhaps I'm lonely, even if I don't typically identify it as such, since I've always been a lone wolf. I've lived alone since 18, happy to be free from family ties. Not once have I felt homesick due to bitter family relations, relishing solitude uniquely fitting for me.

However, absence of all contact is excessive, and fantasies of endless romantic affection betray underlying cravings for warmth. They suggest need denies complete introversion.

## Getting Fired
Last week (4/20 - 4/24), I did no work, not even reaching 24 hours; it was zero hours spent mostly writing blog posts like this.

Given the flexibility of setting hours, no one noticed. But naturally, news came, notifying that I was finished, effectively "fired." This happened today, April 27, at 5:34 PM (though there was a publication delay). Ironically, writing about my current project coincided with its end while typing this—knowing I was now jobless as I wrote.

## Supposed to Be a Story on Enlightenment
Initially, this was meant as a story on "Enlightenment," an understanding episode I experienced on April 8, shortly after starting my three-day job.

On April 1, the first day of work, I began feeling an intense pressure rooted in attachment. Despite longing for the three-day shift with unrestricted hours and no meetings, fulfilling my dream, I wondered why it hurt so much.

After suffering from this issue, I engaged with articles like ["Understanding Enlightenment"](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n99c048aa9f9f). By then, I had already learned Eastern philosophy and Buddhism concepts before April and grasped them intellectually.

However, I hadn't truly understood until reflecting to unravel existing attachments amid harsh struggles. Finally, I realized, "Ah, so this is attachment." Fulfillment didn't alleviate pain; energy came from discarding attachments. Everything fit together after comprehending Eastern ideas and finally realizing personal harmony.

Furthermore, this wasn't what I truly wanted. In March, facing unemployment without a three-day offer, joy crested over sudden opportunity fulfillment, energizing me so much that it felt divine intervention. But it was false, revealed now with the dream expired, eclipsed by looming joblessness and all-encompassing emptiness.

Fetched conditions didn't nurture genuine motivation. True desires elude rationality or logic. When even good workplaces didn't satisfy—expressly quitting despite harmony—then neither promises nor schedules match core needs.

## Realizing I'm Stuck
Overall, I learned that great conditions alone couldn't sustain motivation if excess freedom or sparse relationships built barriers. Yet even overcoming that wouldn't guarantee utopia.

Such roles persisted: past workplaces had balance, still didn't persist beyond honest endeavors. It suggests hired work inherently doesn't fit me. However, my writings don't sustain me, leaving no clear wish to pursue.

Despite seeking three-day jobs—destroying prospects by personal stress and waning enthusiasm rapidly, failing within a month after maintaining initial zeal—April's departure future seems bleak and incredible considering only three weeks passed and didn't involve a five-day schedule.

Enlightenment proved fleetingly apparent, not deeply sustained. I see yet resist ignoring ingrained habits and desires. Life counteracts intentions continuously. Laozi's wisdom never seemed more apt.

## Lost Will to Live
Given sudden contract termination, despite best efforts to expedite, I'll be a NEET for a while. Currently, seeking new work lacks motivation after grand expectations crumbled overnight in my face.

Previously, unscheduled contract losses caused NEET phases, and while such frequent experiences desensitize me, a seemingly perfect setup collapsing quickly leaves bitter reflections nonetheless.

In truth, this isn't my first three-day job experience. Last year, I contracted as hired development with similar terms, modifying systems under deadline without teamwork, even less human connection, sliding similarly despite specifics naturally arising.

Reflecting reveals three-day roles elude me repeatedly. Some could handle independent completion under remote conditions, but I cannot; the supposedly perfect offering ended excruciatingly ironically quicker than anticipated, far surpassing March motivations while ironically faltering sooner than five-day weeks.

Having assumed understanding, realizing inability to shake engrained habits, it's absurd when Laozi's philosophy resounds personally knowing no escape exists and outcomes constantly divert course.

The challenge remains: how to live next. Accompanying lengthy unemployment, debilitating debt (student loans), tax burdens, cavernously await return. Facing such daunting existence, concerns arise if life's worth continuing…
