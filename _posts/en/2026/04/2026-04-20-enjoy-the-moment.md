## The Universe Doesn't Allow Everyone to Aim for the Same Thing
Ultimately, if you're happy now, that's enough. That's the only "right" answer for you.

You might think that if I say this, people will stop working hard and become lazy. And probably, there's no way I can convince you otherwise.

However, from the network of cause and effect in this universe (connections), it's simply not possible, and it shouldn't be, for everyone to strive in the same direction (or a similar one).

If everyone worked hard in their studies and all entered a top university (it doesn't have to be a top university) and all became bureaucrats (it doesn't have to be bureaucrat either), who would grow the vegetables? Who would raise pigs and cows? Who would build shelters from the rain and wind? If everyone went in the same direction, human society couldn't function.

## Absolute Happiness Doesn't Exist
That being said, realistically, it seems that bureaucrats lead a more affluent life (as society commonly believes) than farmers and seem happier.

But is that really true? There are farmers who look happy with their constant smiles. There are bureaucrats who always seem angry, unhappy, and not enjoying life. Whether someone feels happy is something only they know, but even from a distance, you can kind of tell if someone seems happy or miserable. So it's not hard to imagine that sometimes, a poorer farmer can be happier than a wealthier bureaucrat.

I've already discussed this difference multiple times in other articles, so I'll skip it here. But do check out this one 👉 [I Fully Understand How to Achieve Enlightenment, Let Me Explain](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n99c048aa9f9f)

Even so, I acknowledge that, looking at the numbers (as a whole), it might not seem that way.

However, if you adopt this mindset (achieve enlightenment), there's no way that a person in the farming profession can't be happy. Conversely, even if one studies hard, endures struggles, and becomes a bureaucrat, it doesn't guarantee happiness.

## Misery Doesn't Exist
You might think that even if you're happy now, if you neglect what you must do now, the future will be miserable, and it won't mean anything, but that idea itself is a misconception.

What kind of "miserable situation" makes you feel "unhappy"? Is being jobless and running out of money what causes "unhappiness"? Is that really true? Becoming jobless might lead you to accidentally discover a new job, and what you used to dislike might suddenly become enjoyable. If you ignore all those possibilities and just assume "unemployment = unhappiness," isn't that just a misconception?

Or perhaps, by being jobless and failing to find new work, you might accidentally meet your soulmate and become happy since your partner earns well, so you don't need to work anymore (like being a stay-at-home spouse). (Though just because you become a stay-at-home spouse doesn't mean you're guaranteed happiness. It depends on how you define unhappiness.)

From an economic perspective, it's clear that living in a poor African country is harsher than for those living in poverty in Japan. I understand what you’re pointing out. Yet there are unhappy Japanese and seemingly happy Africans. The difference lies in how they perceive unhappiness.

Moreover, what really is unhappiness? Can it be defined? If it can, there shouldn’t be both proud and happy people doing the same job, nor should there be those who dislike it. Similarly, one person finding one job enjoyable while another finds a different job fun shouldn't be possible.

## You Can Only Be Happy in This Moment
The point is, **you can only feel happiness and enjoyment in this moment.** **There's no way, not even in heaven and earth turning upside down, that you can make your future self happy.** The goals and dreams you currently hold might not make you happy even if they come true, and life rarely goes as planned.

If that's the case, doing what you want now, immersing yourself in what you find enjoyable, is the only way to be happy. That continuous repetition of the "doing what I want to do right now (happy) moments" leads to true happiness.

## Everything is Connections
There's definitely something only you can do. You might be the only one who can make the person in front of you happy. And if that person becomes happy, you'll be happy too (helping others ultimately helps yourself).

Without farmers, I couldn't survive. I couldn't write this now. After all, I've never grown the life-essential food with my own hands (so this productiveness isn't limited to just farmers, but also involves butchers and factory workers).

Without bureaucrats, I wouldn’t know when I’d have been murdered. I couldn't write this now. Because I've never made policies to maintain order, like preventing murder (the same applies to politicians and police).

Therefore, I sincerely thank both farmers and bureaucrats. It's because of them that I am who I am today (of course, this gratitude extends to other professions as well). I wasn't living by my own strength. I was sustained by others, society, nature, and the universe.

It's all thanks to connections. Forcing yourself to believe, "I won't be happy unless I have this job," and going against the universe’s causalities (bad connections) causes suffering, eventually making you feel unhappy.

Someone happy working as a farmer might not necessarily become as happy if they turn into a bureaucrat. If that's the case, you should take the utmost pride in being a farmer (or bureaucrat, if that's your path to happiness).

I'm not saying that farmers should only focus on farming or that politicians should only focus on politics. That's too simplistic. If you feel, "Something doesn't fit," changing jobs (or not) is perfectly fine. If you genuinely desire to act, whether rationalistically or without a clear reason, that too is due to connections.

## Optimist? Don't Make Me Laugh
I want to share something, hoping it might help you truly understand this. After reading so far, did you think I'm an overly optimistic or positive freak? That's a huge misunderstanding. Before I came to this understanding (before enlightenment), I was a really, really, really negative monster.

Even in kindergarten, I vividly remember pondering death, seriously thinking about what happens after death, becoming so frightened that it stopped me from functioning.

In middle school (or maybe high school?), without having touched any opposite-sex person other than family, just making a slight contact with a friend made me irrationally fear I might have contracted a sexual disease, and I trembled with fear so much that I went to the health center for tests. I still clearly remember my voice shaking when nervously asking the staff there where to go.

As an adult, the fears continued. Terrified of the long-predicted Nankai Trough earthquake or the Fuji eruption and tsunami that might come with it, I once went to Sydney on a working holiday visa for three months to build a foundation to move overseas (I failed spectacularly).

## A Good Person? Me? Ha
Maybe you got the wrong idea because I wrote about being "truly grateful," and assumed I'm naturally someone who always appreciates and is compassionate.

Wrong. How many times have I thought, "It's only natural to receive from others because this world is so unjust" without a grain of gratitude? How many times have I felt immense hatred towards a pedestrian who bumped into me, imagined torturing them to death countless times...? The times I've imagined murdering outnumber the bread slices I've eaten (no joke). But this only made my own life more miserable.

## Freed from the Curse
But I encountered Eastern philosophy, learned that pre-enlightenment Buddha (Gautama) was also extremely negative, and woke up. Ah, that's what life (and suffering) is all about. There was no unhappiness. There was no evil. I was just labeling things that way.

The moment I realized that, objectively, my thoughts became "positive" and "good," but it didn't matter. I'm neither a positive person nor a good person. On the contrary, I was the opposite. Now, I realize there's no such thing as happiness or unhappiness, no good or evil (they can't be defined), and I've reached enlightenment.

## Words Can't Explain It, and They’ll Always Be Misunderstood
Saying this, I know there will definitely be people who misunderstand. If being happy now is enough, then it's okay to steal if you want money (without thinking of consequences) or rape someone when aroused—of course, that's never the case.

That's because you're clouded by attachments like money or sexual violence, and truly, you can't possibly want to do such things (beyond being unethical or immoral, it's biologically unlikely). In such cases, there must be an attachment somewhere (although sexual desire might be an instinct, rape is not. Just as when money is nothing more than worthless paper, there's no robbery desire).

Perhaps someone might say, "Following your advice messed up my life, how will you take responsibility?" Again, it's a misunderstanding. No matter the "result," if you think "my life is ruined" it'll be ruined, and if you don't think that way (don't define it as such), no matter what happens, your life isn't ruined.

And even without going to such extremes, this can't be comprehended through words. If it could be, anyone reading just one of the many self-help books out there should be freed from suffering.

Similarly, reading this doesn't guarantee understanding (enlightenment). But perhaps, if 100 people read this, at least one might understand. That's the connection leading me to write this.

## In Conclusion
From a modern viewpoint, the idea of "if you're happy now, that's all that matters" might not be easily accepted. You might want to resist it. You might want to argue against it. And for now, that's fine.

However, I hope that you'll tuck it away in the back of your mind, and when the moment comes, have a sudden "Ah!!! So that’s what it was about!!!!" realization. I truly wish from the bottom of my heart that somehow, you'll understand this (achieve enlightenment). You don't have to thank me. If you just live your life to the fullest, that'll make me happy.
