### I Thought I Understood, but I Didn't

Recently, I wrote an article titled "[Let Me Explain How to Achieve Enlightenment, Because I Completely Understand It](https://note.com/noraworld/n/n99c048aa9f9f)." While I indeed understood the method for achieving enlightenment, I realized that I, the writer, haven't actually reached the "state of enlightenment."

Enlightenment is not just about acquiring knowledge or understanding theories and reasoning (cognitive understanding). It refers to an experiential understanding where you feel it with all your heart (non-cognitive understanding). Just like I mentioned in the article, no matter how much a professional pianist verbally explains to you how to play piano well, and even if you completely grasp this knowledge, that doesn't mean you'll actually be able to play beautifully. That analogy was not wrong.

Understanding what it means to "attain enlightenment," to be free from all suffering and to gain an unmatched peace — if that term is used to describe "achieving enlightenment," then I wasn't entirely wrong in understanding what that "opening" meant.

However, I realized that I haven't reached that "state" yet. I understand the method and can somewhat experience it personally, feeling like I've gotten "close" (I feel that if I remember this and stay aware of it in daily life, I won't be consumed by suffering).

But today, I realized that my understanding is not the "state" reached by those who came before. I discovered what kind of trials one must face before reaching that state.

What is necessary to reach the "state" of enlightenment? It is...

"**(Mentally) dying**."

Understanding that was part of what the Heart Sutra described, yet I felt like I'm not ready to face that kind of fear.

### What Is the State of Enlightenment?

If any of the following situations make you think, "No way, I don't want that," then you haven't reached the state of enlightenment (peace without suffering). On the contrary, if you can consider any worst-case scenarios like the ones below (or anything you think "this would be the worst!") and think, "Well, it's fine," then you can say you've reached that state (not limited to the situations below, but in every aspect of life).

* Waking up the next morning as an old person
* Waking up diagnosed with an incurable disease
* Waking up to your beloved partner suddenly saying, "Let's break up"
* Waking up to find all your assets gone
* Waking up and getting suddenly fired from work
* Waking up unable to do anything you painstakingly learned
* Waking up having forgotten everything you struggled to remember
* Waking up to find all your precious things reduced to ashes
* Waking up to find all the cherished photos on your phone lost

Can you lose everything (assets, status, honor, followers, work, loved ones, memorabilia, etc.) and still think, "Well, I can start my life over from scratch"?

Well, you can understand that to think "Well, whatever" even when these happen requires overcoming extraordinary fear. Accepting the loss of what you hold dearest (identity, something as important as life) means a "mental death."

### Letting Go of All Attachments Is Challenging

Indeed, this is "attachment." Knowing that only by letting go of all attachments can you reach a state free from suffering, a state of ultimate peace, led me to realize this. Furthermore, it's up to each person to face it because attachments differ among individuals (hearing people talk isn't enough; you must pluck up the courage to practice to reach it).

No matter what tragedy or misfortune (thought of as so by society or yourself now) occurs, to develop a mindset that allows you to think, "Well, it's fine" regarding those tragedies and misfortunes. To put it succinctly: being able to think, "Well, it's fine" no matter what happens.

This signifies the collapse of self. It means the loss of identity. It means losing the thing most important in your life. It means, mentally, you "die."

#### When "Money Is Me"

Take this case as an example.

Suppose you are distrustful of people and think that the only trustworthy thing in the world (never betraying you) is money. You worked hard and became a billionaire, having total assets of $5 trillion. Could you still think, "Well, it's fine," even if tomorrow the bank's system got hacked and you lost everything?

Here, since the only thing you trust is "money," "My identity = Money." This is the ultimate attachment for this person. Therefore, if one day you truly lose that $5 trillion, and can think, "Well, whatever," then you have achieved a state where nothing brings fear or suffering, gaining ultimate peace.

If you were tied up and threatened that you would be killed unless you handed over the $5 trillion, could you calmly give it away without any attachment? If so, nothing would threaten you anymore.

Well, in this example, it leads to "succumbing to crime," which might spark other discussions, so instead of crimes, consider a situation where your beloved partner or child is critically ill, and it costs a fortune to cure them — would you commit to spending all your wealth without hesitation for the treatment?

Of course, this is about being ultimately attached to money. Just because you can say, "Yeah, I'd spend any amount on my child," doesn't mean you've achieved enlightenment. If money isn't your ultimate attachment, it won't be the same.

#### When "Beauty Is Me"

Another case: suppose you have an exceptional obsession with "beauty," spending a tremendous amount of time, money, and effort every day to maintain your appearance. In this scenario, money might not be your greatest attachment.

But imagine facing an unexpected accident, leaving a prominent scar (especially on the face or other visible areas) on your body for life. Could you still think, "Well, it's fine"?

Here, since "My identity = Beauty," having your body damaged implies suffering equivalent to damaging your value/identity. Not letting go of this attachment, being unable to discard "My identity = Beauty," leads to immense suffering when it's damaged. This isn't limited to "My identity = Beauty"; unless you live without defining any identity, when something about your identity gets damaged, it results in great suffering.

Consequently, you'll start fearing that such things might happen while you live, severely limiting your actions. If taken too far, it leads to extreme behavior restrictions, like, "Outside, there are cars, and you may get involved in an accident (I want to visit a store, but) I'll stay home as much as possible" or "Overexposure to sunlight causes spots (I want to go out and play with friends, but) I'll stay home as much as possible."

Unable to let go of the ultimate attachment, "My identity = Beauty," you fear losing beauty (including ageing), suffer, and also suffer from restricting yourself from doing things you actually want (like going out frequently). A vicious cycle of suffering that begins.

This example focused on women, but men aren't exceptions either. Say you've worked hard to have a beautifully toned, muscular body that other men envy and even competed as a bodybuilder. What if, for some reason, you wake up the next day transformed into either a skinny or very plump body, losing all the results of your blood, sweat, and tears in an instant — could you still say, "Well, it's fine"?

It bears repeating: this applies not only to "My identity = Money" or "My identity = Beauty," but to everything. Unless you live without defining any identity as self, when something you define as your identity gets damaged, immense suffering follows.

### It’s Tough

Letting go of the root cause of attachments (money as the only trustworthy thing or striving for tremendous beauty) means discarding your core, the thing you believed defined your existence. That, indeed, is reaching the state of enlightenment. Discovering that before achieving it, you must face the fear of "the collapse of ego/identity" — a mental death.

It's understandable, really. If someone said, "Throw away what you've believed to be your existence — your life, your reason for living!", naturally, you'd resist, and genuinely throwing it away would be terrifying. It means being "mentally" dead (identity-wise, existentially).

However, I found that overcoming that fear leads to a state free from any suffering, described in the Heart Sutra.

Thinking about it, it makes sense. If you unhesitatingly let go of what you most cherish (the ultimate attachment for you), then what remains to cause suffering? Indeed, if the thing you cherished most is gone, and you feel fine, then you would be unmoved by anything else. It's the ultimate mental strength.

The author realized that they're not yet at that state. Right before jumping off an emotional cliff, I experienced hesitation. So, this is the ultimate attachment.

### Sowa Ka (Svaha)

Die, age, get sick, feel pain, experiencing all sorts of events, and still being able to think, "Well, it's fine." The ultimate "Well, it's fine" mindset is the state of enlightenment.

Of course, not every human in the world has to aim for this. Life is individual. There are those who want to achieve something, even if it involves suffering, or those who don't want suffering but refuse to let go of their core beliefs (the essential identity). That's a valid way of life, too.

This world is impermanent. The things that exist now will inevitably disappear. It’s impossible to ensure something is never lost (never die, never age, never get sick, our loved ones never leave us (never lose a soulmate), never face a financial catastrophe, never see our effort wasted, or our cherished data erased, etc.). Absolute things never disappear do not exist. I realized that accepting this (letting go of such attachments) leads to ultimate peace.

Lao Tzu once said, "If you do not worry about what you are, you can become what you might be." The armor that you call your identity, thinking, "This is truly me," dropping it doesn’t mean you "physically" die in reality. Upon reaching that state, you might truly become your real self (just maybe!!).

**Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā!!!!!**
