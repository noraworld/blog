**Let go of attachments**.

### Declaration of Happiness
Before you achieve enlightenment, make this declaration:

"**I will prioritize my own happiness as my primary value in life from now on**"

This may seem very obvious, but it's important. The reason is that when we hold various attachments in our daily lives, we tend to forget this simple truth.

If the term "happiness" makes you a bit uneasy, you can think of it as "being free from suffering and worries."

The term "declaration" might sound a bit formal, but it's really not hard. Just thinking, "I want to be happy," is enough.

If you have something more important to achieve than being happy, then achieving enlightenment might be (probably) impossible for you. Well, it might be possible, but at least I don't know how, so I can't help.

However, everyone has their own life. You might have something you want to accomplish even if it means suffering... that's a valid life choice. I'm not denying that. If, after achieving your goal, you decide to prioritize happiness or you've worked hard but feel unrewarded, and you're tired and want to shift your goal to happiness, come back when you're ready. It's never too late to aim for enlightenment.



### Understanding Most Suffering Happens in the Mind
This might be shocking to those who are currently suffering:

**Your suffering and worries are actually just in your imagination**.

I understand if you feel like saying, "Don't be ridiculous! You don't know my situation at all, so you can say such irresponsible things." But hear me out.

Suppose, just suppose, that your current suffering or worries are actually just imagined... If that's true, wouldn't it disappear instantly...?

Imagine your friend is watching a movie. The protagonist has a pretty terrible life. The movie is so realistic that your friend gets completely absorbed in it and becomes emotionally invested. They say to you:

"My life is so unlucky! Can you understand my suffering!!?"

If you had a friend like that in front of you, what would you say?

"Hey, relax. Good things happen if you keep going," maybe?

Or would you say, "No, no, no! It's just a movie! You're just watching a movie. It doesn't affect your life at all! Wake up!!"?

What’s happening to you is pretty much the same thing.

#### Experiencing Suffering Happening Only in the Mind
I don't expect you to be convinced with just this. This is just an analogy. Now I'll let you actually experience that your suffering is just your imagination.

First, be alone. If there's someone around, move to a space where you can be alone. Then, close your eyes (although if you close your eyes now, you won't be able to read the upcoming steps, so read through this first and then close your eyes).

Think of a past unpleasant event. Try to recall it vividly, remembering the details and strong emotions it brought, just like when "someone blocked the hallway and bumped into you without apologizing" or "your boss transferred blame for their mistake onto you and scolded you," anything that might bring out negative emotions.

⚠️ **As mentioned earlier, if you’ve experienced traumatic abuse or violence, this exercise is not for you. Please perform it safely with medical guidance or wait until you've recovered.**

You might have felt various negative emotions like anger, irritation, sadness, shame, anxiety, fear, guilt, jealousy, etc.

After experiencing those feelings, stop recalling the event and open your eyes.

Is the "person who bumped into you and clicked their tongue" or "boss who transferred blame" in front of you? No, right? (You’re alone now.) Despite no longer having a subject (a person) causing negative emotions and the event being over, you still felt upset recalling it.

But that’s happening **only in your head**, right? Sure, I instructed you to do so, but even in daily life, unexpected moments will remind you of unpleasant events. But those are **just imaginations**, right? At that moment, the person who made you angry isn't in front of you, right?

In reality, the essence of human suffering is just this. It’s merely imagining things. Your brain is showing you dreams that make you feel uncomfortable. When you wake up, nothing is really there to make you uncomfortable. It’s just your imagination.

It's true that the "person who bumped into you and clicked their tongue" or the "boss who transferred blame" did exist (and that you remember them too). Feeling uncomfortable when it happened is natural. But those events don't continue indefinitely. The "bumping" lasts only around a second and it's said that anger lasts no longer than 10 seconds.

When being scolded by your boss, you might feel discomfort but probably you're more focused on how to get out of that meaningless situation than feeling bad. Actually feeling discomfort might occur after their scolding finishes, right?



### Understanding What You're Attached To
If you’ve read this far and felt, "Oh, so that’s why suffering is imaginary!" and all your suffering and worries vanished, you've reached enlightenment. Congratulations 🎉

However, in reality, most people would think, "If only it were that easy to rid my worries!"

Why do people have worries and suffer...?

Because you **have attachments**.

This is the most important yet the hardest part because **everyone has different attachments**.

The "person who bumped into you and clicked their tongue" or the "boss who transferred blame" are only typical examples of unpleasant events. Anyone hears about these events will say “that’s tough, must've been hard...” and empathize.

However, people's worries are varied and not all shared by everyone as "bad experiences." Just like some people feel fulfillment in the same job where others do not, or successful-looking people are unhappy for reasons not evident on the outside.

So, you can’t say, "This is your attachment," because everyone's is different (which makes it hard). But here are some representative examples. These are "representative examples," so they may not apply to you.

#### Example of a Man
A man said he wanted to make more money.

When asked why he wanted more money, on the surface, he says things like "I want to live comfortably" or "I want a stable life," but from an outsider's perspective, he seems rich enough and living comfortably and stably. But he constantly feels like, “It’s not enough,” “I’m not there yet,” and suffers.

Hearing his life story, you find that he was lonely as a child. His parents worked and had no grandparents to babysit him, so he often played alone. He really wanted to play with his mom and dad more.

So he studied very hard to get good grades. He then showed those grades to his parents wanting praise, "Look, mom and dad! I got such good grades!" But his parents were busy, and while they praised him, they were just bland words lacking heartfelt affection that even a young boy could perceive. He thought, "I need to work harder."

Then he worked very hard at his studies, got into an elite college. After graduation, entered a top-tier company and steadily progressed in his career, giving the impression of leading a smooth, enviable life.

However, his heart felt unfulfilled. He thought, “I have to work harder, earn more…” He felt trapped in a restless routine even when wanting to take a break.

So, what was this man attached to? Money? That might be some part, but fundamentally, he was attached to his past, craving the recognition he didn't receive as a child.

The key is not to deem having a craving for recognition as evil. The desire for recognition is part of human instincts for survival, thus impossible to entirely eliminate. What's important is acknowledging you have this attachment and being able to let it go.

#### Example of a Woman
A woman said she wanted plastic surgery.

She is a very beautiful woman. Very young. Admired by many men, having lots of choices. But from an outside view, it's puzzling why she wants plastic surgery. She says "I don't like the shape of my cheeks" or "my eyes aren't symmetrical" or "this mole bothers me," but people around her think she's beautiful, including those qualities.

But no matter how much she hears praise, she ignores it. One day, she finally went through with plastic surgery, changing everything she didn't like (according to her) and the surgery succeeded.

However, even after the successful surgery and her looks changed, she didn’t feel joyous. Now she said, "I worry my forehead is too broad," and "Maybe I should have higher nose," etc. Eventually, despite being very slim, she felt she should lose weight, tried harsh diets, and fell ill.

Plastic surgeries cost a fortune. She wasn't wealthy, so she cut necessary expenses to fund her in surgeries. She's broke now. But she still wants more surgery… She even considered quitting her current job to work in the adult industry.

Why does she care so much about her appearance?

When you hear about her upbringing, she was quite popular as a student. Boys adored her and treated her nicely but girls despised her and bullied her.

"You think you're all that because you're popular with boys, but you're actually ugly."

"Boys just want to sleep with you, they're not interested in you."

"Because of you, I can’t find a boyfriend. I wish you’d disappear."

The bullying continued through school but ended as she graduated. Now an adult, as time passed, the scars healed, and she acted as if it's nothing from the past and irrelevant to her now.

In this case, the attachment is kind of obvious. Yet human nature interestingly doesn't let the person realize (by thinking it’s all in the past, settling on "I got over it and forgot"), often called “stealth attachments.”

Don't get confused thinking "getting surgery is bad" or "joining the adult industry is wrong." The core issue is why she's not joyful even after achieving these ambitions. Acknowledging the attachment (clinging to forget as it’s painful to think about the past) and letting it go is key.

### Attitude When Finding Your Attachment
You might have more than one attachment. While the core attachment may be few, you may have 20-30 or even more smaller attachments related to it.

Attachments show up in various forms. The core attachment manifests in daily life as small attachments. You don’t need to find them all at once; start recognizing everyday small attachments, unravel them like a chain, and eventually you'll find the core attachment.

For example, just before writing this, I thought, "I have to write this clearly for everyone to understand," which hindered my writing and became an attachment, so I recognized it (there's no requirement for everyone to fully understand my writings) and let it go, gaining energy to move on.

In my case, this is an attachment to perfectionism. Someone else who struggles to write might have a different attachment like "wanting others to understand" which might be obsessive domination. **You can’t determine the cause of attachment based on someone’s behavior's outcome alone.** It requires inner reflection to know what struggling thoughts create their suffering.

Finding core attachments is a persistent task. You may have “stealth” attachments like the women’s example that you're unaware of.

However, of course, unless you recognize your own attachment, you can’t release it. "It’s impossible to release what's unseen."

However, don't think like "I must absolutely find it” or "I should find it soon." That's because **that becomes another attachment**. Allow yourself to go, “Given all this past suffering, it’s natural it takes time to find out. While I live my daily life, I’ll ponder it when relaxed and find it when it comes” with a light-hearted mindset.



### Practicing Letting Go
Once you know your attachment, it becomes easy. Release it. You might wonder, “How do I let it go…,” but you should have declared earlier on:

"**I will prioritize my happiness as my primary value in life from now on**"

If that's the most important value, wouldn't "not forgiving an annoying person" conflict with this value? Distance you from the goal?

If it feels difficult, ask yourself these questions when you sense attachment 👇

* 💡 "In your life, are there things that must be done, achieved or you want so badly, that you should bear with suffering and increase pain instead of happiness?"
* 💡 "Is there any reason in your life why you have to persist in living, even clinging to things?"
* 💡 "Does that attachment bring you happiness? What’s the meaning of keeping something that brings suffering?"
* 💡 "Is there anything more important in life than peace of mind that you have to endure, achieve or pursue to this extent?"

It's alright even if you don't let go of everything at once (thinking "I need to release everything at once!" is also attachment). Recall and ask yourself these any time you feel attachment. Gradually, all your clung thoughts will start to feel ridiculous.

Also, releasing an attachment once doesn’t mean you won’t experience it again. Attachments are old established thought patterns, and they might resurface. But don't worry. Each time simply recognize them with an "Oh, it's you again" and say goodbye. Just like mastering a somersault, letting attachments go requires practice.

You might want to list down your easily ensnaring attachments so the next time they appear, you won't get entangled (watch out, don't get too attached to keeping your list perfectly neat. It’s fine if you can release without those!).



### Pitfalls
Some might feel lighter so far, but **be careful not to create other attachments in this process**.

For instance,

* "I must let go of attachments (otherwise, I can’t be happy)" attachment (attachment to attachment)
* "Having struggled so much in life, I must be happy now (it’s unfair otherwise)" attachment (attachment to happiness)
* "I want this happy feeling to last forever (won’t be happy if it doesn’t)" attachment (also attachment to happiness)
* "Believing in these ideas will make my life mundane" attachment (attachment to fear/resistance)
* "Living so self-centered will make me a bad person" attachment (guilt attachment)
* "Now that I’m free, isn’t it satisfying to look down on those causing suffering over trivial pride?" attachment (superiority/pride attachment)
* "This idea is amazing; others should understand it too" attachment (control attachment)

Some might be confusing, so let’s clarify.

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Happiness"
Don’t misunderstand "attachment to happiness" as not being allowed to wish for happiness. Wanting to be happy is fine; thinking “must be happy!” isn’t. Adopt a light perspective like, "Well, it’s okay not being happy now. I’ve been so miserable, if happiness takes time, that’s fine."

Happiness behaves like a magnet. **Attach to happiness (approach with the same polarity) and it will repel (repulsive force takes over).** **Don't hold on to it (changing the approach, awaiting like the opposite side of the magnet), and happiness comes to you (attractive force draws it).** It’s counterintuitive, but that’s the truth. So, "surrender to happiness."

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Fear/Resistance"
"I have things I need to do, but despite wanting to, I just can’t start... still, letting go of this as attachment, am I just neglecting necessary actions?"

Such thoughts often occupy those with serious or perfectionist tendencies. I understand perfectly as I was (often still am) very much a perfectionist.

But if you think back over your life, weren’t there moments where despite a “got to do it” thing nowhere near completion, you think “Oh well, the worst that happens is it doesn’t kill me” and still eventually completed unexpected tasks?

That’s exactly it! Reasons for procrastination vary by person and situation, but usually, it’s driven by attachments like “must do it or disaster will happen” (attachment to fear of the future) or “must do it perfectly” (attachment to other’s expectations or self-image).

Recognizing as attachment, letting go “Oh well, whatever (if the worst happens, it’s up to how I perceive it)” can often coincide with success. Counterintuitive, yet it’s true.

Don’t expect like, "Thinking ‘Oh well’ means getting things done, so I'll hope for that outcome" because that’s attachment too.

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Guilt"
The "attachment to guilt" involves the widespread misbelief “Acting self-centered makes you a bad person.” Prioritizing your own happiness and acting out of it differs from saying your desires selfishly. That’s “I want to control others” attachment. That breeds suffering (not happiness).

At the core, deliberately hurting others without attachment seems **biologically unnatural** (not ethics or morality). **Hurting others is due to attachment to money or a background need**. **Attachments hurt both you and others**. It brings Lose-Lose.

Practicing benevolence becomes natural for those with peace of mind (don’t pressure yourself to do so; "you must wish this way" is also attachment). **Happiness, unlike divisible goods, amplifies when shared**. It results in Win-Win.

Being kind to feel good is totally fine in my view. "Kindness is not for others" as the saying. Always judgment from outsiders (society) arises. That’s unnecessary (fear of what others think is a mere imagination).

Likely, criticizers are filled with attachment. "Nice to gain public appraisal deserves punishment” attachment is within (if you’re calculatively benevolent, that’s attachment!). Kindness isn’t obligation; it flourishes naturally once liberated (wished for).

Conversely, if you can’t desire helping others (yet), that’s fine. Don’t worry if time passes. Repeating again, “must be kind” is another attachment.

Perhaps, you’re naturally kind without realizing. Isn’t that great? It prevents the trap of “pride in altruism” attachment before it arises. How lucky!



### Be Careful Judging Others or Yourself
Lastly, here’s something to consider. Judging people by labels like “They’re kind” or “They’re not kind” also leads to attachment. On what basis do you say “that person” (or yourself) is “kind/kindless”? Is there anything that’s always and purely "kind" (according to you)? Is that merely imagination?

Why are people not kind? Because they’re filled with attachment. (It’s not strictly accurate by later theories, but) I think fundamentally, all people can be “kind.” But as they live and see life’s mud (attachment), it obscures their inherent kindness (lotus).

Speaking in a dichotomy of "kind/devoid of kindness" results in new attachments, like “I won’t be nice to the unkind (I’ll be mean)” which causes a chain reaction of reciprocation and suffering. Acknowledging “Person A is a kind person” is already misleading. “Person A is person A” doesn’t properly represent anyone (including yourself).

Because, sure? Anyone claiming “I have a bright personality” would have to smile calmly through seeing their family murdered in front of them. If a bit of sadness seeps in, the “bright personality” collapses. It’s self-denial. Hey, I’d like meeting someone who keeps their “set personality” unyielded throughout their life.

The argument “But I’m just speaking generally about most scenarios compared to others” might surface, but even so, does labeling "I’m a ○○ personality" contribute to your happiness?

If you proclaim “I’m relatively bright,” where does it come from? How does it not stem from comparison, potentially giving birth to attachment, which ordinarily weighs you when experiencing a down period (when you’re not “bright”)?

Conversely, being “a dark person,” needless to say, can hinder perceived adequacy. “I’m a dark personality, that's why social ties suffer,” becomes a self-fulfilling perception. Besides, “dark personality” isn’t always destructive (could be beneficial at times). Assessing yourself means nothing helpful if it leads to suffering or inappropriate sense of self (for others too).

Honestly, even writing this surprises me. All my life I thought, "In such unfairness, embracing self-centered conduct won't fetch harm." Once an adult, I sometimes consciously hurt people out of jealousy (attachment to envy’s kind). In company meetings, no less (weirdly, the victim’s name was "Satoru"...!!).

Sure, I feel bad toward those individuals but don’t dwell in self-hate over my mistake. "I’m a bad person" isn’t applicable anymore. If it were, inadvertently performing an act of gratitude would contradict self-identity (grinned "Whoa! I got thanked!! Not a bad person at all!!! " lol).



### Achieving Enlightenment Doesn’t Mean Never Getting Down
This is something I also want to mention: achieving enlightenment doesn't mean you'll never feel down again. If you're thinking, "Really?" just picture this situation:

No matter how strong your mental resilience, if you "never step outside, curtains drawn, subsisting on energy drinks and fast food, sitting all day, endlessly playing games in a dark room" for weeks or months, you’d go crazy.

While you likely wouldn’t willingly endure such conditions without attachment (and this is extreme), even in somewhat similar situations like being forced to endure extreme inhuman work conditions, as said early on, you need to fix that first.

And even if not as severe, if you're thinking, "My mentality’s strongest, I’ll never feel down! (It’s abnormal if I do)" it becomes attachment and may result in suffering. Adopt a stance like, "Well, feeling down's human" and take breaks. Before you realize, you might forget what had you down entirely.



### Lastly
Even after reading to the end without identifying own attachments, understanding the words but not feeling settled... I believe it's normal.

That's the essence. Achieving "enlightenment" doesn’t come from reading many books on Eastern philosophy and stacking complex theories and knowledge. It involves deeply feeling a **sense of realization**.

Put simply, despite explaining in words, without achieving realization, you may just find it an interesting perspective, or contrasting, once you experience realization (even from a single book, or ideally this post), you grasp **complete understanding**.

To explain further, suppose I’m a professional pianist, saying "Play piano like this to excel" doesn’t make listeners proficient pianists instantly.

And as common perception goes, anyone declaring "I achieved enlightenment" might sound dubious (I think so myself while saying it).

Yet, this is unlike those. **When one day you think, "Hey, I’ve reached enlightenment," that stands as your own enlightenment**. It’s abnormal thinking standard for anyone. Recognizing and letting that attachment go, enabling yourself to declare understanding brings enlightenment. Precise words may differ, yet what does it matter? Is there anything more important than such actualization?

Enlightened, to me, signifies feeling "**Despite unknowable future events, grounding today refrains me from feeling misery or considering life unhappy, possessing undeniable reassurance**". It’s not deity-faith or belief-magic, or an assurance chant, or attachment of avoiding unhappiness. It just brings secured life and earned internal trust perception.

Apologies for acting knowledgeable about everyone’s burdens. In truth, I don’t fathom them. Again, attachments uniquely differ per person, and I don’t know your life journey, unable to specify your case. "A young bloke thinking he knows," your admonishment stands valid.

Sorry addressing achieving enlightenment knowingly too. I may not truly (fully) grasp it. I still get irritated sometimes. And there’s potential to hurt others too.

But, you know, none of that bothers me anymore. I’m neither excusing with "What’s wrong considering that so’s me?" nor I’m giving up on anything. Actually, it feels alive; thoughts like “If I must suffer through life, what’s its meaning anyway?” flood my mind.

Overreacting occasionally and getting attached somewhat is fine (“a lot” too, as long you notice and start afresh). Key isn’t never attaching henceforth, but lightheartedly acknowledging, "I attached!" then thinking "That binds suffering, better release it soon."

I cannot "lead each of you to enlightenment" as individuals (attachments differ, and not everyone genuinely seeks relief). Yet, assisting aspirants even slightly motivates me to write this. Helping a bit, if such be, is my utmost joy.
