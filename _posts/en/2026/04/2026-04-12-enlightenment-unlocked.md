### Conclusion
I don’t think sugarcoating things with vague explanations is helpful, so I’ll get straight to the point.

**Let go of attachments**.

If you fully understand after reading just this, you don’t need to continue. Great job! 👏

If not, read on 👇



### Happiness Declaration
Before seeking enlightenment, make this declaration:

“**From now on, I place my happiness as the most important value in my life**”

It may sound too simple, but it’s crucial. We easily forget this in daily life when we get attached to various things.

If "being happy" sounds too awkward, you can replace it with "liberation from suffering and worries."

Although it might sound grand, it’s not difficult. Even just thinking, "I want to be happy," is enough.

If there is something you value more than being happy, opening enlightenment might be (probably) impossible for you. Well, it might be possible, but I don't know how, so I can't help you.

But people have different lives. Some might have things they want to accomplish even if it causes suffering. That kind of life exists and is fine. If you achieve that purpose and then want to prioritize happiness, or you’ve been trying hard for a goal but feel unrewarded and exhausted, and wish to shift to prioritizing happiness, come back here. It’s okay, it's never too late to seek enlightenment.



### Understanding That Almost All Suffering Happens in the Mind
Here’s something shocking for those suffering from something right now.

**Your suffering or worry is actually an illusion**.

I understand if you’re angry and want to say, "You don’t know anything about my situation to say such irresponsible things!" But just hear me out.

"Suppose, just suppose, your current suffering or worry is indeed an illusion…" Imagine that. If it’s true, don’t you think that suffering would disappear immediately?

For example, imagine a friend watching a movie. The protagonist lives a tragically miserable life, and the movie is so realistic and well-made that your friend totally empathizes with the protagonist. Your friend then laments to you, "My life is so unhappy! Do you understand my suffering!!?"

If you had such a friend in front of you, what would you say?

Would you say, "Hey, calm down, good things will come eventually if you keep living"?

Or would you say, "No, no, no! It’s just a movie? You’re just watching a movie? It’s not affecting your life at all. Wake up!"

What’s happening to you is exactly the same.

#### Experience Suffering Occurring Only in the Mind
I don’t expect you to be convinced just by this. It’s just an analogy. So now, I want you to actually experience that your suffering is just an illusion.

First, find a place to be alone. If there are people around you, move to a space where you can be alone. Then close your eyes (but don’t close them now, or you won’t be able to read the following steps; read them through first and close right before doing the exercise again).

Recall a bad incident from the past. Try to remember it in detail and with as strong emotions as possible. Something like, "They clearly blocked the way and bumped into me without apologizing, and even clicked their tongue," or "My boss made a mistake but blamed and scolded me," would be good examples. Anything that likely evokes strong negative emotions is suitable.

⚠️ **As mentioned at the beginning, if you’ve experienced trauma like parental abuse or domestic violence, consult a doctor and proceed safely before attempting this.**

You probably felt emotions like anger, irritation, sadness, embarrassment, anxiety, fear, guilt, jealousy, among others.

After experiencing those emotions again, stop recalling the event and open your eyes.

Now, in front of you, is there a "pedestrian who bumped into you and clicked their tongue" or a "boss who shifted responsibility"? No, right? (You’re alone now). Even though there’s no target for the negative emotions and it’s something past, didn’t thinking back make you feel uncomfortable?

But that’s **just occurring in your head, isn’t it?** I asked you to think back by directing you, but in daily life, don’t you sometimes remember unpleasant events suddenly? However, **that’s just an illusion, right?** At that moment, there’s no one in front of you to be angry at, is there?

Actually, human suffering is just this: it’s all in your imagination. Your brain is just making you see disturbing dreams. When you wake up, the things causing the discomfort don’t exist. It’s just an illusion.

Indeed, there were the “pedestrian who bumped into you and clicked their tongue” and the “boss who shifted responsibility.” It’s a fact that they occurred (and that you remember them). It’s natural to feel discomfort the moment those things happen. But those events don’t continue endlessly. It only takes about a second to “bump into” someone, and human anger usually lasts only up to around 10 seconds.

When being scolded by a "boss shifting responsibility," you might be using your head more on "How can I quickly escape this meaningless lecture?" rather than experiencing discomfort. Isn’t the strong discomfort felt after the boss’s scolding completes?



### Knowing the True Nature of Your Attachments
If reading up to here made you think, "Oh, suffering is only an illusion!" and all your suffering and worries vanished, you have achieved enlightenment. Congratulations 🎉

In actuality, most people think, "If it were that easy, I wouldn’t struggle so much!"

So, why do people suffer and worry about something…?

**It’s because there’s attachment**.

This is the most crucial and hardest part because **each person's attachments are different**.

The "pedestrian who bumped into you and clicked their tongue" or the "boss who shifted responsibility" are typical examples of unpleasant events. When heard, everyone can sympathize with "It must have been hard."

However, everyone has different worries, and not all are shared unpleasant experiences. Even people doing the same job might derive different levels of satisfaction. Or successful people might still look unhappy.

So I can’t definitively say, "Your attachment is this," (and that's why it's difficult), but here are some typical examples. They are only "typical examples," so it may not apply to you.

#### A Man's Example
A man said he wanted to earn more money.

When asked why he wants more money, on the surface, he said things like "I want a richer life" or "I want a stable life." But from others' perspectives, he already has enough money for a rich and stable life. But he feels, "It's still not enough," and "This isn't where it ends," causing him suffering.

Looking at his life, you find out he felt lonely in his childhood. His parents worked, and he had no grandparents to care for him, so he often played alone. But, he really wanted to play more with his mom and dad, to have more attention.

Therefore, during school, he often studied hard and tried to get good grades. He hoped to show these to his parents, saying, "Look, I scored excellent! Isn't that amazing?" and he wanted them to praise him. But his parents were busy, and though they praised him, their words felt empty even for a young boy.

So he thought, "I haven't tried hard enough," and continued to study hard, entering a top university and, after graduation, a top company. He steadily worked and got promoted, living what of many would call a perfect life.

But, he’s not satisfied.  He feels he has to work harder and earn more money, leading a frustrating life.

What is this man's attachment? Is it money? Maybe partly, but more fundamentally, it's the yearning for parental recognition he never received, an attachment to past approval.

It’s important to note that having a desire for recognition isn’t bad. It’s a natural human survival instinct, so you can’t completely get rid of it. What’s necessary is to recognize that you have an attachment to that recognition and let it go.

#### A Woman's Example
A woman said she wanted cosmetic surgery.

This woman is beautiful and young, constantly pursued by many men. Others wonder why on earth she would want cosmetic surgery. She says things like, "I don’t like the sharpness of my jaw," or, "My eyes are uneven," or how a mole bothers her. Others think she's beautiful with it all.

But she doesn’t listen, and one day, she gets cosmetic surgery. She addresses everything she found unappealing, and the surgery was a success.

However, even after the successful surgery, she didn’t seem happy. Now she begins to think, "My forehead is too broad," or, "My nose could be taller," then thinks she should slim down even though she's already thin, leading to an unhealthy diet and illness.

Cosmetic surgery is costly. She wasn’t wealthy, and she spent her savings on surgery. Now, with no savings, she wants more surgery, considering quitting her job and entering the adult industry.

Why does she care so much about appearance?

Talking with her about her past reveals she was also popular as a student. Boys liked and were nice to her, but girls were envious and bullied her, saying things like, "You may seem cool to boys, but you're really ugly," or "They’re nice to you just for your body, not because they care," or "You're keeping a boyfriend away from me. Why don't you just disappear?"

The bullying continued through school, yet with graduating and becoming a working adult, these old wounds healed, and she dismissed them as past issues with no current relevance.

Well, it’s clear what her attachment was. But often, people can’t recognize their own obvious attachments—things they buried and wanted forgotten.

To clarify, cosmetic surgery or joining the adult industry isn’t being criticized. Even if you're doing what you want, if it doesn't fulfill you, it’s essential to recognize the attachment (not wanting to remember the past experiences) and let go.

### Mindset When Identifying Your Attachments
Your attachments might not be just one. While you might have a core attachment, there could be 20–30 or more accompanying attachments.

Attachments manifest in many forms. Core attachments present smaller, daily life-level attachments. Instead of trying to find everything at once, recognize small attachments in daily life, and uncover the core attachment as you untangle them.

For example, just before I wrote this, I thought, “I have to write in a way that everyone reading will understand,” which was an attachment, and it slowed my writing. Recognizing, "Oh, this is an attachment (I don’t have to make everyone completely understand),” I let it go, regaining my motivation to write.

For me, this was a perfectionism attachment. Someone else's hesitation in writing might stem from an attachment to controlling others' understanding, which is a different attachment. **One’s behavior doesn't define their attachment's cause**. It requires looking inside for why someone struggles with “writing well."

Finding core attachments is a painstaking task, as the woman's example illustrated, where you might not yet recognize something as an attachment ("a stealth attachment").

However, obviously, until you find your attachments, you can’t let them go. “Letting go of invisible things” is impossible.

But here, don't think “I must find them” or “I should find them quickly,” because **those become new attachments**. Think, "I've been struggling, so it’s no wonder I can't find them quickly. I’ll live normally, and maybe in a random moment, I’ll think about it and find them.” Keep it light.



### Practice Letting Go
Once you identify your attachments, it's simple. Let them go. You might think, "How do I let go?" Remember earlier, you declared:

“**From now on, I place my happiness as the most important value in my life**.”

If this value is the most crucial, doesn’t “not forgiving someone you’re mad at” contradict this value? Doesn’t it move you away from your goal?

If tough, ask yourself these questions when feeling attached: 👇

* 💡 "In your life, are there things you must endure, accomplish, or want to do so badly that you'd sacrifice happiness to experience suffering?"
* 💡 "Is there a reason you must continue living with attachments in your life?"
* 💡 "Does that attachment bring you happiness? What meaning does maintaining it, while bringing suffering, have in your life?"
* 💡 "What is so important that you must endure, accomplish, or do, more than living peacefully?"

You don’t have to let everything go at once (thinking “I must release everything at once” is also an attachment). Recall this and self-question each time you feel an attachment. Eventually, the things you were attached to will seem trivial.

Also, letting go of an attachment once doesn’t mean you’re forever free from it. Attachments return as ingrained thinking habits. But that’s fine. Every time they appear again, simply notice, “Oh, here it is again,” and bid farewell. It’s practice, like mastering a skill doesn’t happen in a single attempt.

It might help to make a list of attachments you easily fall into so you don’t get trapped when they surface again (but don’t stress about making it perfect—if you can let go quickly without a list, that’s OK).



### Pitfalls
While lightening some people’s hearts, **beware of creating different forms of attachments** in the process.

For example:

* “I must let go of attachments (or I can’t be happy)”—an attachment to attachment.
* “I must be happy given how much I’ve struggled (it’d be strange otherwise)”—an attachment to happiness.
* “I want this feeling of happiness to last forever (I’d hate if it doesn’t)”—an attachment to happiness (again).
* “Believing such things makes my life trivial”—an attachment to fear or resistance.
* “Being this self-centered will make me a bad person”—an attachment to guilt.
* “I’ve moved past such personal suffering, but there are others out there still—those fools!”—an attachment to superiority or pride.
* “This is such a great way of thinking; others must understand”—an attachment to control.

Let’s address some misunderstandings.

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Happiness"
About "attachment to happiness," the message isn’t that you can’t wish to be happy. However, thinking “I must be happy” is an attachment. Keep it light with, "Well, if I’m not happy immediately, that’s fine. After all, with all my past suffering, it's natural not to be right away.”

Happiness is like a magnet. **When you attach to happiness (approaching with the same poles), happiness moves away (repelled).** **When you’re not attached (holding with opposite poles), happiness comes to you naturally (attracted).** Ironically, this is true; be “at peace with peace.”

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Fear and Resistance"
“I have something I should do, but I keep procrastinating… But if I let it go, what if I neglect something truly important…”

This perhaps resonates with serious or perfectionist people. I, too, was (and sometimes still am) a perfectionist, so I relate.

But reflecting on life, while some things I thought needed urgent action didn’t move forward, others that were like, "Well, if I don’t do it, I’ll still live,” just somehow got done. Do you have experience with that?

That's it! While procrastination’s reason varies, usually it's “I must, or it'll be a disaster (attachment to future fear),” or “I must do it perfectly (attachment to others’ expectations or self-image nurtured by position),” causing the brakes.

Recognizing the attachment and thinking, “Well, whatever (even if this happens, I decide my happiness),” releases the brakes unexpectedly. Though ironic, it’s true.

But be cautious not to expect, “I'll think it’s okay and naturally do it, so let's hope that happens.” That’s also an attachment.

#### Misunderstanding "Attachment to Guilt"
Additionally, "attachment to guilt" is a common misunderstanding ("Being self-centered will make me a bad person"). Prioritizing your happiness isn’t "pushing your way through selfishness." Wanting "control or outcome from others," leads to attachment and suffering (it doesn’t bring happiness).

Intentionally harming people without any attachment sounds biologically **unnatural**. Doesn’t seem any **biological** rationale to it. Hurting for money stems from attachment to money, or something shadowed behind it. **Attachments hurt yourself and others.** It’s Lose-Lose.

People with room mentally naturally wish to help others (but if you don’t, don’t feel bad either. Thinking "I should want to help" becomes an attachment). **Happiness isn’t giving and reducing yours but increases both giver and receiver.** It’s Win-Win.

Helping for self-satisfaction doesn’t seem wrong. "Kindness isn’t an ethical or moral duty but an act done naturally, without restrictions, when freed from attachments."

In reverse, if (still) you feel no desire to be kind, that’s fine. If the moment doesn't come soon, don't be discouraged. Repeating, "You feel you must be kind" and that becomes a new attachment.

So maybe you are being kind without thinking kindly. That’s great too. You never noticed and avoided "I feel kind, and others aren’t—bad,” likely destroying that possibility early. Lucky you.



### Be Careful With Judging Yourself or Others
Moreover, I’d like to mention, comments like "That person is kind" or "This person isn’t" are attachments too. What makes you think "that person (or even yourself)" is "kind or not"? Is there something in every situation, and with every person, that’s always “kind (as you see)”?

Why can’t people be kind? It’s being caught up in attachments. Although not strictly accurate, it evokes the idea that inherently, people are “kind.” In life, they know the world’s mud (attachments), and they become enshrouded, concealing the lotus (kindness).

Because people talk about others in binary terms of "kind" or "not kind," attachments form, leading to chains of suffering. "Mr. XX is a kind person" is a misbelief. “XX is a XX person,” truly can’t be said of anyone (even yourself).

Think of it this way: If someone declares, "I’m a bright person," then if their family were killed before their eyes, they couldn’t be upset, always smiling. Having a bit of sadness means their "bright person" self has collapsed—it’s self-denial. I’d be impressed to see someone maintain a stated personality for life without exceptions.

You might respond, "No, it’s a generalization, more frequent than others," but suppose you self-assess as being a certain type, does it help you be happier?

If "being brighter than someone else," is only said through comparisons with others, attachments form. As everyone experiences emotional highs and lows, if you portray yourself as “happy,” any time you're not, it intensifies your damage (it equates to self-denial).

Alternatively, thinking, “I’m a gloomy person,” leads to self-fulfilling prophecy— you believe it causes failed interactions. An “unhappy personality” isn’t always negative (there can be positives).

Anyway, “Judging yourself or others as XX” never benefits living more pleasantly. Judging people (including yourself) as labels like "XX" leads to attachments.

Honestly, I surprise myself writing this. I lived thinking "With a world this unfair, it's okay to behave self-centeredly." Entering work, I felt entitled to spite someone who evoked envy (an attachment). I even did so during a company meeting.

Sure, I realize it was wrong, but I’m not consumed by "I’m a bad person…” anymore. Simply acknowledging that “I’m a bad person” doesn’t hold. If it did, doing something good would consequently be self-denial.

### Enlightenment Doesn’t Mean Never Feeling Down
Finally, enlightenment doesn’t mean you'll never feel down again.

Thinking, “Enlightenment makes me unsinkable!” creates attachments and suffers otherwise. Keep a mindset like, “I’m human, I’ll sometimes be down,” and do distractions. When you notice, you’ll be out of it and won’t even recall the cause.

### In the End
If you haven’t yet figured out your attachments, you read till now, understanding the words without fully getting it... That’s essential.

Enlightenment isn't reached by studying volumes on Eastern philosophy and building complex theories. It’s through "experiencing realization" in your heart.

Phrase it differently, words pile up without realization leading nowhere, whereas experiencing realization fully understands from a single source.

For an analogy: If I were a pro pianist describing, “Play the piano this way,” not everyone will become a pro pianist just hearing it.

Typically, society thinks, "Anyone claiming enlightenment or understanding is dodgy"—I agree. 

But enlightenment for you is when **you have a moment thinking, "I might be enlightened,"**. Everyone’s individual experience is differently peculiar. Spotting, identifying, and releasing your attachments allows true enlightenment and understanding.

To me, enlightenment means “**no idea what future awaits. Yet, whatever occurs, holding this path assures no suffering or thoughts of an unhappy life."** Not influenced by fictitious deities or murmurs of self-assurance, neither an attachment to “not being unhappy.” It’s grasping something real with felt serenity for life.

Apologizing for presuming everyone else’s pain, but I don’t truly know. Everyone's reliance varies, stories unknown to me. Claiming enlightenment as I apologize again, because perhaps I haven’t reached true understanding either. I get irritated still and may hurt others.

However, refreshed enough not to care too much about that is a relief. Not defying condemnation over "evil actions," nor giving up on life. Welling excitement as "now my life truly begins" pervades.

Consequently, reacting (slightly or more) to attachments is okay. If you realize then restart, that’s enough. The importance isn’t “never attaching again,” but to detach lightly on realization to avoid discomfort.

I can’t “enlighten” everyone (different attachments, not everyone wishes for release). Writing to help those desiring assistance was the aim. Any perceived benefit brings immense joy.
