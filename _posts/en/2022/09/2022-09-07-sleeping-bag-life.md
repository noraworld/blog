I've recently started living with a sleeping bag instead of a bed, and I want to share my thoughts about it.

## Benefits

There are several benefits to getting rid of your bed and switching to a sleeping bag.

Note that these benefits are specifically about living with a sleeping bag instead of a bed. I'm not going to discuss how comfortable sleeping bags are themselves.

### Takes Up Less Space

Beds need a specific spot to stay in.

Imagine you're living in a studio apartment that's 10 tatami mats big for 100,000 yen a month, and your bed takes up one tatami mat of that space.

That means you're paying one-tenth of your rent, or 10,000 yen, just for the space where your bed sits each month.

On the other hand, sleeping bags don't take up much space. Depending on the type, if it has a bag with a hanging strap, you can just hang it somewhere when you're not using it, and it takes up zero space. Even without a strap, it wouldn't take up as much room as a bed would.

If you need a 10 tatami mat room just for the bed, getting rid of the bed means you can live comfortably in a 9 tatami mat room. Although it's not a straightforward calculation, generally speaking, smaller rooms come with lower rent, so you can find a cheaper place.

### Easier to Move

Transporting a bed is a tough job, especially if it's a big one.

Sleeping bags are easy to carry, allowing you to change your sleeping place freely. Without a bed, moving becomes much simpler.

### Less Laundry Hassle

This is only true for sleeping bags that can be washed all at once, but they're easier to clean.

With a bed, you have to take off the sheet from the comforter and mattress, wash them, and then put them back on. But with a sleeping bag, all you have to do is wash it, making the process much simpler.

## Drawbacks

Of course, it's not all good news. Let's talk about some drawbacks.

### Sleeping Comfort Isn't Great

You might guess this, but compared to a bed, a sleeping bag is not as comfortable.

However, I personally think this is something you can get used to. In my experience, having used both expensive and cheap items in life, you'll find that people adapt. It's often more cost-effective to get used to cheaper options.

For example, whether you use an expensive $300 keyboard or the built-in keyboard on a laptop, your typing speed doesn't really change once you get used to it. Anyone arguing that it's slower hasn't really adapted, in my opinion.

Also, whether a sleeping bag is comfortable can depend on the floor's hardness. If the floor is too hard, adding a mat can make a big difference.

### Packing It Away Is a Hassle

With a bed, you just make the bed quickly, but with a sleeping bag, you have to roll it up and pack it into its bag.

This doesn’t take much time, but it's still a bit of work. However, this is only because beds usually stay out permanently. Packing a bed away every day would be much more of a hassle. 

So, maybe that’s not really a drawback of sleeping bags compared to beds. Being able to pack it away is more of an advantage.

### Getting In and Out Is a Hassle

With a bed, you just move the duvet aside with your hand and easily get in and out. But with a sleeping bag, you have to wiggle out, which can be a bit tedious.

Since I'm pretty laid-back, I've just been using the sleeping bag like a comforter. It's still warm enough and has been comfortable for me so far.

## Counterarguments

Reading this far, you might have some objections, and I want to address a few.

### Isn't it a bit much to say you’re paying for bed space since you use the bed every day?

You might think so, sure.

But think about it. You don't use your bed all day. Even if you sleep for 8 hours, that's only one-third of your day.

So, you'd still pay about 6,600 yen of that monthly 10,000 yen just to have the bed there.

I'm not dissing living with a bed, I’m just offering a different way to see things.

### Won't sleeping in a sleeping bag lower your sleep quality?

Honestly, no, it doesn’t change.

I measure my sleep quality with a tracker, and my scores are about the same whether I sleep in a bed or a sleeping bag.

Granted, I haven’t been using the sleeping bag for too long, so the data isn’t complete. But performance-wise during the day or from my own experience, not much has changed.

There’s even some research showing that changing mattresses doesn’t affect sleep quality:

https://kaken.nii.ac.jp/file/KAKENHI-PROJECT-23650457/23650457seika.pdf

This study focuses on different mattresses, not beds versus sleeping bags, so it’s not a solid conclusion but still useful info.

In my opinion, better sleep on pricy beds might just be a placebo. You just feel like you’re sleeping better.

Still, sleeping on a hard floor might hurt your back or waist, so you’d need to sort that out.

If you’ve wanted to try living with a sleeping bag but worried about sleep quality, this might be just the news you need.

### It's hard to get rid of a bed if you have a family

True, this is trickier when you’ve got a family. You'll need to convince your partner.

And if you're single, look for a partner who’s cool with sleeping bags.

## Conclusion

This article is aimed at those curious about sleeping bag life but unsure about making the change. It doesn’t put down living with a bed.

Even if you hadn’t considered it before, I hope reading this makes you think that living with a sleeping bag could be a possibility.
